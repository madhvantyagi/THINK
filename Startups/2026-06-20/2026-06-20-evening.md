## 1. Avea Robotics

**Money.** Avea is a YC Spring 2026 accelerator/seed company, so the verified financing is YC's standard $500,000. Its June 1 launch says Sentinel reached **$18,000 in monthly recurring revenue** and helped deploy nearly **30 robots**; customer names, contract duration, gross retention, and the split between software and integration revenue are not public. This is the strongest current commercial evidence in this set. [YC company and launch record, June 1, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/avea-robotics)

**Technology — disclosed.** Sentinel is a hardware-agnostic teleoperation layer for 6-DoF and 7-DoF arms, ALOHA-style systems, industrial arms, and humanoids. Avea claims latency as low as 10 milliseconds, immersive depth viewing, haptic feedback, and up to six full-HD video feeds. Integration uses a robot's URDF and joint-control API; customers run a Docker container beside the robot. The system can collect joint states, RGB streams, point clouds, and other sensor data, while video and data streams are end-to-end encrypted and stored locally. [Avea product site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://www.avearobotics.com/)

**Technology — analyst reconstruction.** A local bridge likely normalizes robot kinematics and sensor messages, applies safety limits, compresses video, and exchanges commands with an operator client over a low-latency transport. Motion retargeting maps VR controller or operator pose into joint or end-effector targets, while inverse kinematics, collision limits, velocity bounds, and watchdogs prevent unsafe commands. Ten-millisecond latency is plausible on favorable network paths but needs a definition—one-way input latency, command round trip, or motion-to-photon—and tail latency matters more than a minimum. Internet jitter, packet loss, camera encoding, operator geography, and robot-control frequency can destabilize manipulation.

The difficult engineering is safe cross-hardware control, not streaming alone. URDFs do not capture every actuator limit, controller quirk, tool, collision surface, or safety PLC. Haptics also require force/torque sensing and stable feedback under delay. The same deployment produces intervention traces that can become imitation-learning or failure-recovery data, creating a stronger moat than teleoperation software if Avea owns data rights and labels interventions well. Commodity WebRTC, ROS, containers, and VR hardware reduce the initial software barrier.

**Founders.** Vikram Vadrevu wrote flight software for NASA and Department of Defense satellites, directly relevant to embedded reliability and low-latency control. Ary Indarapu brings the deployment focus but discloses less technical history. The missing team capabilities are industrial functional safety, field support, and enterprise robotics sales.

**Verdict — 8.2/10, Medium-high confidence.** Real revenue and deployed robots distinguish Avea. The decisive questions are p95/p99 latency, intervention success rate, supported safety architecture, revenue concentration, and whether teleoperation data improves autonomy.

## 2. Clawvisor

**Money.** Clawvisor is a YC Spring 2026 accelerator/seed company with the standard $500,000 YC investment and no disclosed outside round. Its cloud product offers a free trial; the open-source repository showed about **240 stars, 30 forks, and 695 commits** when accessed. No paid customer, active-agent count, revenue, or enterprise contract is public. [YC company and launch record, May 7, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/clawvisor) [GitHub repository, accessed June 20, 2026](https://github.com/clawvisor/clawvisor)

**Technology — disclosed.** Clawvisor is a gateway between an agent and external APIs. The agent declares a task purpose and requested service/action pairs; a human approves that scope once. Each later tool call passes through hard restrictions, task-scope checks, and a per-request approval fallback. Credentials remain in a vault and are injected server-side only for approved execution. Optional LLM subsystems perform task-risk assessment, intent verification, and extraction of structural facts such as IDs or email addresses so later calls can be checked against earlier results. The repository documents 249 evaluation cases, 14 service adapters, SQLite or PostgreSQL deployment, hosted and self-hosted modes, rate limits, audit logs, and session or standing tasks.

**Technology — analyst assessment.** Separating deterministic action allowlists from probabilistic intent checks is the correct architecture. The hard part is semantic authorization: deciding whether parameters truly serve an approved purpose without allowing prompt injection, scope laundering, or confused-deputy behavior. LLM verification can fail open or block legitimate work; attackers can exploit ambiguity in task descriptions. Chain-context checks help constrain entity substitution but do not prove that a requested action is safe.

The strongest moat could be a high-quality authorization-policy language, adapters with precise schemas, production attack traces, and independently measured false-allow/false-block rates. Vaulting, OAuth proxying, approval queues, and audit logs are otherwise familiar security components. Deployment also requires process isolation: Clawvisor's own README warns that an agent sharing its host can bypass the gateway by reading the database or environment. The repository explicitly says the software is experimental, unaudited, and should not be the sole safeguard for critical systems.

**Founders.** Eric Levine previously co-founded identity-verification company Berbix, sold to Socure for a reported $70 million, and led Trust & Safety engineering at Airbnb. That is exceptional founder-market fit. Single-founder bandwidth is the main organizational risk for security review, integrations, enterprise sales, and incident response.

**Verdict — 8.0/10, High confidence.** The system is unusually inspectable and correctly frames agent permissions as purpose-bound authorization. Commercial readiness depends on an independent security audit, red-team results, measured policy error rates, and proof that enterprises will place Clawvisor in a privileged execution path.

## 3. Intelligence Factory

**Money.** Intelligence Factory is a YC Spring 2026 accelerator/seed company with YC's $500,000 standard investment and no additional disclosed financing. It says it is deploying in warehouses, grocery stores, and data centers, but names no customer, robot count, paid pilot, revenue, or task-level performance. [YC company and launch record, May 29, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/intelligence-factory)

**Technology — disclosed.** The company collects human demonstrations in "data factories" using custom gloves that capture what operators see, do, and feel. It trains general-purpose manipulation models and retargets human motion across robot hardware ranging from five-finger hands to simpler grippers. This is a vertically integrated data strategy: capture rich demonstrations, map them into robot action spaces, train policies, and deploy them in varied physical environments.

**Technology — analyst reconstruction.** A useful glove system likely records hand pose, finger articulation, force or tactile signals, head or wrist video, and timing. Calibration aligns the human hand, objects, cameras, and robot frames. Retargeting then solves a constrained optimization problem: preserve task-relevant contacts and trajectories while respecting a robot's different kinematics, reach, joint limits, gripper geometry, and control rate. Training may combine behavioral cloning, vision-language-action policies, diffusion policies, or reinforcement learning from successful and failed rollouts.

The difficult problem is not collecting demonstrations; it is producing data that transfers. Human hands have far more dexterity and sensing than most grippers, and an action that is natural for a person may be mechanically impossible for a robot. Policies also fail under lighting changes, clutter, deformable objects, unseen packaging, and recovery situations. A strong system needs synchronized multimodal data, precise success labels, failure interventions, hardware normalization, and evaluation by task family rather than impressive demos. Commodity gloves, cameras, foundation vision encoders, and robot arms are available. Defensibility would come from diverse real-world trajectories, robust retargeting, deployment feedback, and a policy layer that generalizes across embodiments.

Inference latency and safety are material. Manipulation policies must run quickly enough for closed-loop correction, detect uncertainty, hand control to a human, and prevent unsafe forces or collisions. Data collection is labor-intensive, while model training and simulation add GPU cost; customer economics depend on successful picks per hour and avoided human labor, not model novelty.

**Founders.** Yash Sinha has robotics training from ETH Zurich, prior founder/CTO experience, and simulation work in Formula 1. Jalaj Shukla has manipulation, vision-language-action, controls, and industrial robotics experience from Dimensional, Blue Sky Robotics, Applied Materials, Ansys, UPenn GRASP, and DRDO. The team is highly aligned; the gap is disclosed manufacturing-scale field operations.

**Verdict — 7.9/10, Medium confidence.** The data-and-retargeting thesis is technically coherent and the team is strong. The rating is capped until the company publishes task success rates, intervention frequency, supported embodiments, deployment duration, and customer economics.

## 4. Modern

**Money.** Modern is a YC Spring 2026 accelerator/seed company with the standard $500,000 YC investment and no disclosed additional round. Its site reports anonymous enterprise outcomes: 62% lower ticket handling, 10 hours saved per new hire, 74% faster response, and 50% of tickets closed within 30 days of deployment, rising to 70% by day 60. These are company claims without named customers, sample sizes, baseline definitions, revenue, or retention data. [YC company and launch record, May 11, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/modern) [Modern product site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://getmodern.ai/)

**Technology — disclosed.** Modern automates help desk, access, hardware, onboarding, offboarding, security, procurement, HR, finance, and legal workflows across chat and existing IT systems. It explicitly separates agent reasoning from code-based actions. An agent interprets the ticket and chooses a path; approved deterministic workflows, derived from SOPs or natural-language descriptions, execute the changes. Every action is checked against policy and logged. Integrations include ServiceNow, Zendesk, Microsoft Graph, Google Workspace, Workday, SAP, Salesforce, Slack, and Meraki. The company claims SOC 2 Type 2, ISO 27001, SAML, regional deployments, and advanced permissions.

**Technology — analyst reconstruction.** Requests likely enter through channel adapters, are classified and enriched with employee, device, identity, and policy context, then routed to a workflow graph. The crucial boundary is between proposing a plan and mutating systems. Access grants, employee termination, payroll, and device wipes require deterministic preconditions, approvals, idempotency, rollback where possible, and reconciliation after partial failure. A natural-language workflow builder must compile into a typed, reviewable graph rather than execute free-form generated code.

The hard engineering is integration state and policy fidelity. HRIS, identity, SaaS, device, and ticket systems disagree, change APIs, and fail mid-workflow. Audit evidence must show exactly who authorized what, which policy version applied, and whether downstream state matches intent. Commodity LLMs, workflow engines, and connectors make the initial product reproducible; the moat would be a reliable connector library, policy templates, execution traces, and accumulated mappings from manual fixes into safe automation. ServiceNow, Jira Service Management, Freshservice, Moveworks, and MSPs are formidable distribution competitors.

**Founders.** Seb Poole previously built and scaled Frankie/Unmind with major enterprise customers. Aleks Tomovski spent 15 years on developer platforms, including Wix Code and Canva's internal developer platform. This is strong enterprise distribution plus platform engineering fit.

**Verdict — 7.8/10, Medium confidence.** Modern has the right deterministic-execution boundary and unusually relevant founders. Named references, independent security evidence, connector reliability, gross margin for Modern Managed, and proof that automation rates persist after deployment would raise confidence.

## 5. Astraea

**Money.** Astraea is a YC Spring 2026 accelerator/seed company with YC's standard $500,000 investment and no disclosed outside round. Its site references work with a top-three pharma company, a top-ten pharma company, a global biotech, and other unnamed organizations. It claims 30–50% faster biometrics cycles, 99.8% validated SDTM/ADaM/TFL outputs, and production R/SAS code with cell-level parity to references. No customer name, contract, revenue, study count, or independent validation report is public. [YC company and launch record, May 13, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/astraea) [Astraea product site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://www.tryastraea.com/)

**Technology — disclosed.** Astraea ingests protocols, raw clinical data, study metadata, SAPs, CSRs, and literature. Specialized agents generate or map eCRFs, SDTM, ADaM, Define-XML, analysis variables, tables, listings, figures, Pinnacle 21 reports, SDRGs, ADRGs, and submission packages. The platform describes standards-native traceability, human approvals, controlled execution, data desensitization, and alignment with HIPAA, GDPR, 21 CFR Part 11, GxP, CDISC, FDA, and EMA workflows.

**Technology — analyst reconstruction.** The defensible architecture is a regulated compiler. LLMs can extract study intent and propose mappings, but deterministic schema validators, controlled terminology, derivation rules, statistical code, and reproducible execution must produce the final artifacts. Every output should trace to source fields, protocol clauses, transformation versions, reviewer approvals, and code hashes. Statistical programming must run in isolated, versioned R/SAS environments with regression tests and double-programming or risk-based QC for critical endpoints.

The hard problem is semantic correctness. Raw trial data is messy; visits, adverse events, concomitant medications, and protocol deviations have study-specific meaning. A syntactically CDISC-compliant dataset can still encode the wrong clinical interpretation. Models must handle amendments, missing data, inconsistent units, blinded treatment, and regulator-specific expectations without silently normalizing away ambiguity. Commodity models can draft code and documents; moat comes from validated mappings, reusable study metadata, audit infrastructure, and a corpus of corrected outputs. Liability and validation cost may force substantial expert services, constraining software margins.

**Founders.** Joshua Wang brings math, computer science, and prior production AI work. Sanmay Sarada brings Baylor clinical data engineering, fetal-medicine research, and CS/biology training. The fit is promising but neither biography shows senior biostatistics leadership or direct ownership of an approved NDA/BLA submission.

**Verdict — 7.6/10, Medium confidence.** Astraea targets expensive, structured work where deterministic validation can constrain AI. Confidence requires named study references, protocol-to-output error analysis, audit evidence, inspection outcomes, and clear separation between automated claims and human consulting.

## 6. AgentPhone

**Money.** AgentPhone is a YC Spring 2026 accelerator/seed company with YC's standard $500,000 investment and no additional disclosed financing. It says teams around Google's Agent Development Kit, Replit, YC, Sim AI, LangChain, and Alchemy are building with the product. That demonstrates developer access, not necessarily paid production usage. No active-number count, call volume, revenue, carrier margin, retention, or customer contract is disclosed. [YC company and launch record, May 15, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/agentphone) [AgentPhone product site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://agentphone.ai/)

**Technology — disclosed.** One API provisions U.S. or Canadian numbers and exposes voice and messages through a unified webhook. Incoming calls are transcribed in real time; an agent replies with text; AgentPhone performs text-to-speech. It provides Python and Node SDKs, REST, an MCP server, conversation threading, real-time transcription, and hosted voice-agent behavior. The product also positions numbers as agent identities for calls, SMS, verification messages, and 2FA workflows.

**Technology — analyst reconstruction.** AgentPhone likely sits above one or more carriers or CPaaS providers, normalizing number provisioning, SIP/PSTN call events, SMS delivery, speech-to-text, text-to-speech, and agent session state. The developer value is a stable event model that hides separate telephony, media-streaming, compliance, retry, and billing systems. Real-time voice requires streaming audio, voice-activity detection, interruption handling, low-latency transcription, agent inference, synthesis, and barge-in without losing conversational state.

The difficult parts are operational and regulatory. Carriers filter application-to-person messaging; U.S. campaigns may require registration and consent. Outbound AI calls face robocall, recording-consent, caller-ID, and impersonation rules. Number reputation, spam labeling, fraud, SIM-swap-like abuse, voicemail detection, emergency calling, regional routing, and deliverability can dominate model quality. 2FA is especially risky: platforms may prohibit automated receipt or virtual numbers, and concentrating verification messages creates a high-value account-takeover target. Commodity Twilio-like primitives make a basic API reproducible. A moat would require superior deliverability, compliance automation, agent-specific identity/reputation, fraud controls, and cross-channel state.

**Founders.** Meet Modi previously built WhatsApp agent infrastructure serving 280 million businesses at Meta; Manav Modi helped grow the Vogue app from roughly 100,000 to more than one million users. This is strong telecom/messaging and consumer-product fit.

**Verdict — 7.5/10, Medium confidence.** The abstraction is useful and founder fit is strong. The business needs verified production volume, abuse controls, carrier economics, consent enforcement, data-retention policies, and evidence that it is more than a thin CPaaS wrapper.

## 7. Framewise Health

**Money.** Framewise is a YC Spring 2026 accelerator/seed company with the standard $500,000 YC investment and no disclosed outside round. It markets to hospitals, specialty groups, pharma, medical-device companies, and clinics, but provides no named production customer, patient count, contract, revenue, adherence improvement, readmission reduction, or randomized outcome evidence. [YC company and launch record, May 11, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/framewise-health) [Framewise product site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://www.framewisehealth.com/)

**Technology — disclosed.** Framewise reads diagnoses, medications, allergies, language, and discharge plans through FHIR R4 connections to EHRs such as Epic, Oracle Health, Athenahealth, and others. It generates personalized education videos in more than 75 languages, has clinicians review them, and sends a link by SMS before discharge. The site claims HIPAA and SOC 2 compliance, a signed AWS business-associate agreement, end-to-end encryption, and audit logging. The launch also describes collecting comprehension or engagement signals and returning them to care teams.

**Technology — analyst reconstruction.** A safe pipeline should ingest only necessary FHIR resources, normalize medications and diagnoses, retrieve institution-approved instructions and authoritative drug content, generate a structured script, translate it, run terminology and contraindication checks, obtain clinician approval, synthesize video/audio, and record the exact content version delivered. The patient-facing layer must support low bandwidth, accessibility, captions, language preference, and simple comprehension checks.

The hardest issue is clinical fidelity across languages and literacy levels. Translation quality alone is insufficient: dosing, red-flag symptoms, follow-up timing, uncertainty, and culturally appropriate explanations must remain exact. Generative video adds risks around lip synchronization, avatar trust, numerical errors, and patients mistaking education for personalized medical advice. Human review improves safety but can become the throughput bottleneck and make economics service-heavy. The moat could be an approved content graph, correction data, EHR integration, and measured outcome improvement. FHIR adapters, translation, avatar video, and SMS delivery are commodity components.

Security risk is high because medical records flow into content-generation and delivery systems. SMS links need strong but usable access controls; audit logs, retention limits, consent, revocation, and prevention of PHI leakage into model providers are essential.

**Founders.** Tane Kim left Brown's BS/MD program after exposure to more than 500 patient interactions across a dozen institutions. David researched computer vision at Brown and worked at a voice-AI startup. The team has relevant exposure but lacks disclosed clinical-safety, hospital-integration, and enterprise-security leadership.

**Verdict — 7.3/10, Medium-low confidence.** The workflow is concrete and technically feasible. The score depends on prospective outcome evidence, translation-error rates, clinician-review time, EHR deployment burden, and proof that personalization improves care rather than merely engagement.

## 9. Pentagon

**Money.** Pentagon is a one-founder YC Spring 2026 accelerator/seed company with YC's standard $500,000 investment and no disclosed additional round. Its site claims more than **8,500 persistent agents, 3,500 autonomous agent conversations, and 8,000 completed tasks**, but does not state whether these are unique users, internal tests, free desktop usage, or paid enterprise deployments. No revenue or named customer is public. [YC company and launch record, May 20, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/pentagon) [Pentagon product site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://www.pentagon.run/)

**Technology — disclosed.** Pentagon Studio gives agents a shared spatial workspace, folders, roles, teams, typed channels, group chats, task tracking, status reports, hierarchical skills/tools, persistent memory, and event-driven communication. Humans can see active, waiting, or idle agents and define permissions at the folder, tool, and action level. Enterprise deployment may be hosted, VPC, or on-prem. Hardware-level virtual-machine isolation is marked "coming soon."

**Technology — analyst reconstruction.** The likely core is a persistent orchestration service with an event bus, per-agent session state, shared artifacts, task DAGs, message routing, tool registries, and memory retrieval. Event-driven messaging can reduce waste compared with agents polling one another, but coordination overhead can still explode as teams grow. A manager agent needs bounded delegation, idempotent task assignment, conflict detection, budgets, deadlines, and a clear rule for escalating uncertainty to humans.

The product's main technical risk is confusing communication with correctness. More agents can duplicate work, propagate a false assumption, overwrite shared artifacts, or create circular delegation. Persistent memory can preserve errors and sensitive data. Useful validation would compare multi-agent teams with a strong single agent under equal token, time, and tool budgets, measuring task success, cost, coordination failures, and human interventions. Spatial UI, chat, queues, and vector memory are commodity. Defensibility requires a reliable coordination protocol, evaluation corpus, observability, access control, and accumulated organizational templates. The lack of current sandboxing is material if agents run code or access production systems.

**Founders.** Edgar Pavlovsky previously built marginfi, a Solana lending protocol, a builder community, a trading firm, and logistics software, after roles at Uber and Goldman Sachs. That demonstrates high agency and systems exposure. Single-founder security, enterprise support, and product breadth are significant risks.

**Verdict — 6.9/10, Medium-low confidence.** Pentagon has a coherent control-plane concept and measurable usage claims. It needs paid-customer evidence, cost-adjusted evaluations, real sandboxing, memory-governance controls, and proof that agent teams outperform simpler orchestration.
