## 1. Apollo Atomics

**Money.** Apollo is an accelerator/seed-stage company backed by YC's standard $500,000 investment; no additional priced round is public. Its website claims **20 GW of signed letters of intent**, but does not name counterparties, deposits, prices, or cancellation terms, so this is pipeline evidence rather than bankable revenue. The company says it has tested a working demonstrator and is targeting a fuel milestone before July 4, 2026; neither claim has an independent test report attached. [YC launch, May 27, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/launches/QXj-apollo-atomics-the-modern-nuclear-company) [Company site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://www.apolloatomics.com/)

**Technology — disclosed.** Apollo retains conventional pressurized-water-reactor fundamentals—light water, low-enriched uranium, and established component supply chains—while replacing the large steam generator with a proprietary compact steam generator. It claims the component is 20 times smaller at the same thermal power, enabling reactor packages of 10, 50, and 300 MWe that are factory-built and truck-transportable. Its site also claims passive safety, deployment in under 24 months, and eventual electricity at 3¢/kWh. These are targets, not demonstrated plant economics. The 2027 milestone is a 1 MW demonstrator; therefore today's evidence is component-level rather than an operating power reactor.

**Technology — inference and gaps.** The core engineering problem is heat-transfer density under nuclear pressure, temperature, corrosion, vibration, and fatigue constraints. A smaller steam generator needs substantially more effective heat-transfer area per volume without unacceptable coolant pressure drop, flow instability, fouling, tube wear, or inspection difficulty. The full plant still requires containment, pumps or natural-circulation paths, shielding, control rods, turbine equipment, emergency heat rejection, cybersecurity, and licensed operating procedures. Factory production can reduce site labor, but it does not remove NRC design certification, environmental review, fuel qualification, quality assurance, or utility interconnection. The moat could be patented geometry plus manufacturing and validated thermal-hydraulic data. The major validation gap is whether compactness survives safety margins and maintainability at scale; a non-nuclear demonstrator cannot answer that.

**Founders.** Assil Halimi has unusually direct fit: an MIT nuclear-engineering PhD, reactor-design and fuel-optimization patents/publications, reactor operations at Engie, design work at EDF, and experience with Westinghouse, NuScale, and Rolls-Royce. Drew Walker adds manufacturing/operations and prior electric-boat startup experience, although his nuclear depth is lower. The team is credible for component invention and customer development; it has not yet disclosed regulatory leadership or an experienced nuclear manufacturing organization. [YC founder profiles, accessed June 20, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/apollo-atomics-inc)

**Verdict — 8.4/10, Medium confidence.** The design attacks a real bottleneck with a narrower technical change than exotic-fuel SMRs. The score depends on thermal-hydraulic test data, NRC pathway, qualified manufacturing, and converting LOIs into financed projects.

## 2. Zibra Labs

**Money.** Zibra is accelerator/seed stage with YC's $500,000 standard investment and no disclosed outside round. It is initially selling HPC clusters to quantitative trading firms for large backtests, but names no paying customer, pilot, revenue, or deployed cluster. Its traction is founder credibility and an explicit performance target, not commercial proof. [YC launch, May 18, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/launches/QPY-zibra-labs-hpc-for-quant-trading-firms-running-backtesting-at-scale)

**Technology — disclosed.** The target system spans **100 to 50,000 mixed CPU/GPU nodes**, as many as five to 6.4 million parallel tasks, spot instances across regions and providers, and under 50 ms task-dispatch and scheduling overhead. Workloads run in the customer's cloud or on-prem environment. Zibra says the same engine can support backtesting, reinforcement-learning rollouts, robotics simulation, molecular dynamics, batch inference, and long-running agents. Its founders previously led work on Ray and built LinkedIn's Venice, Liquid, and Espresso data systems. [Company site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://zibralabs.ai/) [Ray repository, accessed June 20, 2026](https://github.com/ray-project/ray)

**Technology — inference and gaps.** The likely architecture is a distributed control plane that decomposes parameter sweeps into tasks, places them by data locality and accelerator type, leases capacity from multiple providers, checkpoints task state, and reschedules work when spot nodes disappear. At this scale, scheduler throughput is only one constraint. Object transfer, dependency tracking, backpressure, metadata durability, stragglers, cold starts, cross-zone egress, quota fragmentation, and reproducibility can dominate. Quant customers also require strict isolation: code, market data, model parameters, and intermediate results must not leak through a multi-cloud control plane.

Ray, Kubernetes, Slurm, cloud batch services, and commercial HPC schedulers are commodity alternatives. Zibra's plausible differentiation is a purpose-built low-latency scheduler with first-class heterogeneous spot capacity and operational knowledge from Ray. The hard question is why this becomes a new platform rather than improvements contributed to Ray or a managed orchestration layer around it. The sub-50 ms claim also needs a defined measurement: median versus tail latency, centralized versus multi-region dispatch, dependency-free tasks versus stateful DAGs, and behavior during node churn.

**Founders.** Ibrahim Rabbani and Zac Policzer have exceptional distributed-systems fit: large databases at LinkedIn, software operating across hundreds of thousands of machines, and technical leadership on Ray. That lowers core execution risk. The gap is domain sales and production support inside secretive quant organizations, where buying cycles, security review, and workload migration are difficult.

**Verdict — 8.1/10, Medium-high confidence.** This is a technically credible team attacking measurable infrastructure waste. The architecture could be defensible, but no external benchmark or live cluster proves the stated scale, latency, or fault behavior.

## 3. Expanse

**Money.** Expanse has YC's $500,000 standard investment and no disclosed outside financing. It offers a two-week cluster assessment followed by a paid pilot decision, but has not named a production customer, revenue, or measured savings from a customer cluster. NVIDIA Inception membership is ecosystem support, not financing or customer validation. [YC launch, May 4, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/launches/QCF-expanse-unlock-wasted-gpu-capacity) [Company site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://expanse.sh/)

**Technology — disclosed.** Expanse deploys inside a customer's cloud or on-prem cluster and says code and telemetry stay within that network. It predicts resource fit and failure risk at submission time, diagnoses failed jobs, and suggests code or configuration changes. The product passively observes workloads, then produces a capacity report showing effective utilization and recoverable capacity. The founders cite prior research in multimodal HPC resource prediction, graph-neural-network prediction of Slurm queue waits, decentralized training, speech-model optimization, and infrastructure at quant funds and national supercomputing centers.

**Technology — inference and gaps.** Inputs likely include job manifests, requested versus consumed CPU/GPU memory, accelerator utilization, I/O, network counters, queue state, exit codes, framework logs, code or dependency fingerprints, and hardware topology. Models can predict peak memory, duration, queue delay, and failure classes before scheduling. A safe deployment should keep hard admission controls deterministic and use learned predictions as recommendations or bounded overrides; an inaccurate under-allocation can destroy long jobs, while over-allocation merely preserves today's waste.

The difficult part is counterfactual validation. Expanse must show that a suggested smaller allocation would have completed within the service-level objective, not merely that historical utilization was low. Workloads drift as code, datasets, batch sizes, kernels, and models change. Multi-tenant clusters also create feedback loops: changing one job's resources changes queue times and placement for every other job. Commodity telemetry, Grafana, Slurm accounting, Kubernetes autoscaling, and cloud cost tools can identify waste; defensibility requires workload-specific prediction quality and a growing cross-job failure corpus. Microsoft's published observation of low deep-learning GPU utilization validates the problem, not Expanse's solution. [Microsoft Research study, accessed June 20, 2026](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/publication/an-empirical-study-on-low-gpu-utilization-of-deep-learning-jobs/)

**Founders.** All four founders have unusually aligned HPC, ML systems, and quant-infrastructure experience. The team is technically broad enough to build data collection, modeling, and cluster integrations. Its commercial gap is proving value without demanding intrusive code access or months of workload history.

**Verdict — 7.9/10, Medium-high confidence.** Strong problem-team fit and an on-prem data boundary make this credible. The rating is capped until a customer publishes recovered GPU-hours, false under-allocation rates, and sustained throughput improvement.

## 4. Madrone

**Money.** Madrone is YC accelerator/seed stage with the standard $500,000 investment and no public outside round. The company has built a **1 kW prototype** and was constructing a **100 kW prototype** at launch. It names no data-center pilot, purchase order, or revenue. Claims that each efficiency point creates $2.6 million of annual GPU revenue per 100 MW are scenario calculations, not customer results. [YC launch, May 12, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/launches/QL1-madrone-chiller-less-cooling-for-desert-data-centers)

**Technology — disclosed.** Madrone places a modular water-cooling system outside the data center and targets 20°C supply water without mechanical chillers. It says a different thermodynamic process cools toward the dew point and uses 30% less power and water than a chiller-plus-cooling-tower configuration in Texas conditions. Electronics, heat exchangers, and control software are designed in-house. The intended use cases are new hyperscale construction and retrofits constrained by grid permits. [Company site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://www.madrone.cool/)

**Technology — inference and gaps.** The wording suggests an indirect evaporative or desiccant-assisted dew-point cycle, but the exact process, working fluid, exchanger geometry, and water loop are undisclosed. The system likely uses staged air streams and heat exchangers so evaporation cools process water without introducing moisture into the facility loop. Control software must optimize fan and pump power, approach temperature, water consumption, fouling risk, and changing ambient wet-bulb conditions.

The hard engineering is not achieving an attractive laboratory coefficient of performance; it is retaining it through dust, mineral scaling, biofouling, 45°C ambient heat, variable humidity, pump failures, and year-round uptime requirements. A 100 MW facility cannot tolerate an immature cooling controller taking accelerators offline. The unit economics depend on heat-exchanger area, water treatment, fan/pump energy, maintenance labor, redundancy, installation footprint, and whether backup chillers remain necessary. Existing evaporative, dry, liquid-to-chip, immersion, and heat-reuse systems create strong competition. Madrone's moat would be validated heat-exchanger IP plus field reliability and manufacturing cost, not "AI" controls.

**Founders.** Erik Meike brings Apple power/thermal hardware experience plus robotics and automated PCB work. Akshay Trikha brings materials modeling and production ML from QuantumScape. That is good prototype fit, though neither public biography shows hyperscale facility operations, water chemistry, or HVAC manufacturing at this power level.

**Verdict — 7.8/10, Medium confidence.** The wedge is physically meaningful because cooling competes directly with compute for grid capacity. The 100 kW prototype, humid-climate performance, water-quality tolerance, and failover architecture are the decisive next evidence.

## 5. Infera

**Money.** Infera has YC's $500,000 standard investment; no additional round is disclosed. It reports pilots with Boston-area academic laboratories and core facilities but names no institution, paid contract, instrument count, or experiment throughput. This is real workflow access, though still pilot rather than revenue evidence. [YC launch, June 2, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/launches/Qdt-infera-control-lab-instruments-with-natural-language)

**Technology — disclosed.** A scientist describes an experiment in natural language. Infera asks clarifying questions, checks inventory and instrument state, represents manual and automated steps, generates vendor-specific scripts for programmable equipment, executes runs, retrieves data, performs analysis, and preserves protocols, exceptions, and prior validated runs. The site depicts protocol parsing, volume and collision checks, deck validation, human-in-the-loop steps, signed audit records, and instrument-ready exports. [Company site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://www.infera.bio/)

**Technology — inference and gaps.** The credible architecture is a compiler, not an unconstrained agent. An LLM converts prose and documents into a typed intermediate protocol; deterministic validators enforce units, reagent compatibility, deck geometry, device capabilities, inventory, timing, and safety constraints; vendor adapters emit scripts; an orchestrator handles execution and event logs. Every translation should retain provenance back to the source protocol, and unsafe ambiguity should stop compilation rather than be resolved probabilistically.

Instrument heterogeneity is the hard part. Vendors expose inconsistent APIs, proprietary file formats, firmware behaviors, and calibration assumptions. Manual steps create state the software cannot directly observe. Reproducibility requires exact versions for methods, reagents, instrument configuration, environmental conditions, and data transformations. Commodity LLMs can draft protocols, and laboratory information-management systems can track samples; Infera's defensibility would come from a validated device-adapter library, constraint engine, and accumulated execution traces. The central risk is liability: one subtly wrong unit conversion or plate mapping can invalidate weeks of work or damage scarce samples.

**Founders.** Chloe Sow has built research software and medical devices across Harvard Medical School, Brigham and Women's, Fred Hutch, and PNNL. Troy Zhang has computational-neuroscience training, cancer publications, an agtech patent, and prior company-building experience. The team has authentic laboratory exposure. It still needs deep quality-system and instrument-integration capacity to sell beyond academic pilots.

**Verdict — 7.7/10, Medium confidence.** The compiler framing is stronger than a generic lab agent. Durable value depends on deterministic validation, adapter breadth, and evidence that automated runs match expert-authored controls.

## 6. Lattice Health

**Money.** Lattice is a one-founder YC seed-stage company with the standard $500,000 investment and no disclosed additional financing. Its site and launch ask hospitals and imaging vendors to request pilots; no named deployment, contract, revenue, or monitored model count is verified. Dashboard figures should be treated as product illustrations unless tied to a customer. [YC launch, June 10, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/launches/Qid-lattice-health-monitoring-and-governance-for-deployed-medical-imaging-ai)

**Technology — disclosed.** Lattice is a read-only observer beside hospital imaging infrastructure. It consumes existing HL7 and DICOM signals, monitors confidence distributions, population stability index, subgroup disparity, latency, radiologist feedback, and possible silent model updates, then creates role-specific daily PDF reports. It claims on-prem or private-cloud deployment, strict PHI-redaction before egress, deterministic narration without an external model API, institution-controlled signing keys, offline signature verification, and chain-of-custody evidence bundles. [Company site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://www.latticehealthai.com/)

**Technology — inference and gaps.** A sidecar architecture reduces operational risk because care continues if Lattice fails. The ingestion layer must normalize AI result objects, DICOM metadata, work-list state, radiologist reports, and model/version identifiers across vendors. Statistical monitors compare current distributions with approved baselines, while a report generator signs immutable summaries and preserves the underlying evidence.

The difficult issue is ground truth. Agreement with a radiologist is not necessarily clinical accuracy; readers can be biased, disagree, or see the AI output before reporting. Outcomes may arrive months later, and subgroup sample sizes can be too small for reliable daily conclusions. PSI can detect distribution change but not whether that change harms sensitivity or specificity. Silent-update detection from output distributions may confuse a model change with scanner, protocol, or population change. Lattice therefore needs causal incident investigation, multiple-testing controls, confidence intervals, and careful selection-bias warnings. Incumbent PACS vendors, AI marketplaces, and hospital analytics teams can build dashboards; signed evidence, cross-vendor normalization, and low-PHI architecture are the more defensible pieces.

**Founders.** Christine Park combines neurosurgery training, a Duke MD/MEng, and a Stanford computer-science PhD path—excellent clinical/technical fit for imaging governance. Single-founder execution is the material gap: integrations, security review, clinical statistics, enterprise sales, and support are too broad for one person.

**Verdict — 7.5/10, Medium confidence.** The product is unusually specific about security and workflow. It must prove that its metrics predict clinically meaningful degradation and survive hospital integration rather than merely producing compliant-looking reports.

## 7. Arlo Industries

**Money.** Arlo has YC's $500,000 standard investment and no additional public round. It reports active demonstrations with Ukrainian military users and relationships in the United States, Ukraine, Finland, and Estonia, but discloses no procurement contract, unit sale, revenue, or paid pilot. Battlefield access is useful product discovery, not yet commercial validation. [YC launch, May 26, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/launches/QWQ-arlo-industries-we-track-drones-and-missiles-without-radar)

**Technology — disclosed.** Mentat is a passive distributed sensor mesh intended to track small aerial threats in three dimensions without emitting, making nodes harder to locate than active radar. Arlo describes low-cost nodes, passive optical sensing, edge compute, mesh networking, off-grid embedded systems, and long-range communications. It positions the network as a sensing layer feeding interceptors and counter-UAS systems rather than an interceptor itself.

**Technology — inference and gaps.** Multiple electro-optical or infrared nodes likely detect candidate objects locally, time-stamp bearings, share track hypotheses, and triangulate position and velocity through multi-view geometry. A fusion service must associate detections across cameras, reject birds and clutter, maintain tracks through occlusion, and estimate uncertainty for downstream weapons or alerts. Accurate clock synchronization, surveyed sensor position, optical calibration, secure mesh routing, and weather-aware detection are foundational.

The company says tracking accuracy rises "exponentially" as nodes are added; that is not a generally valid property and needs measured error curves. Passive optical systems lose performance in fog, cloud, glare, darkness without thermal sensors, camouflage, and low-contrast backgrounds. Long-range identification also collides with lens size, resolution, cost, power, and data bandwidth. The moat can be field data, calibrated fusion software, resilient networking, and deployment doctrine. Commodity cameras, edge accelerators, trackers, and radios are available, while radar and RF-detection vendors can fuse their own passive sensors. Required evidence is detection range by target size, false alarms per hour, 3-D position error, latency, weather envelope, and performance under jamming or node loss.

**Founders.** Deo Arlo studied mechanical engineering and robotics at Technion, patented a prior robotics invention, and has direct conflict-region exposure. The disclosed team includes autonomy and military expertise, but the full leadership and prior air-defense delivery record are not public.

**Verdict — 7.4/10, Medium-low confidence.** Distributed passive sensing has strong cost and survivability logic. Sparse sensor specifications and no quantified field results make the current claims difficult to underwrite.

## 8. Aseon Labs

**Money.** Aseon is YC accelerator/seed stage with the standard $500,000 investment; no outside round is disclosed. It is building pilot hardware and recruiting a founding robotics team, but names no autonomous-vehicle operator as a customer or partner. Its website explicitly says third-party AV imagery does not imply affiliation. Claimed reductions of up to 50% in reset cost, 65% in downtime, and $50,000 in annual revenue uplift per vehicle are modeled targets. [YC launch, May 11, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/launches/QKQ-aseon-labs-robotic-pitstops-for-self-driving-cars) [Company site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://www.aseonlabs.com/)

**Technology — disclosed.** The Aseon Pod is a compact, distributed micro-depot intended to charge, clean, and inspect autonomous vehicles inside their operating zones, reducing empty travel to centralized depots. Public material does not disclose the charging interface, cleaning mechanisms, robot configuration, sensors, supported vehicle geometries, throughput, or footprint.

**Technology — inference and gaps.** A practical pod needs reliable vehicle localization and docking, charger or connector manipulation, exterior wash and sensor cleaning, cabin access and interior cleaning, tire/body/sensor inspection, consumables handling, and a fleet API that schedules resets without creating queues. Computer vision can flag damage or contamination, but safety-critical sensor validation may require calibrated targets and diagnostics from the vehicle itself. Mechanical subsystems face dirt, vandalism, weather, fluids, and varied vehicle designs.

The largest architecture risk is standardization. If every fleet has different charge ports, door controls, sensor placements, cleaning requirements, and secure APIs, each pod becomes custom capital equipment. Throughput also determines economics: a cheap reset is irrelevant if one failed robotic operation blocks the station during peak demand. Unit cost includes urban land, utility upgrades, hardware depreciation, water and waste handling, maintenance visits, and spare capacity. Aseon's moat could be site network density, fleet integrations, and operational learning, but AV manufacturers or charging networks can vertically integrate the same workflows.

**Founders.** George Kalligeros and Dan Keene previously built Pushme's 5,000-location battery-swap network across 40 cities before acquisition by TIER-Dott. Kalligeros later led a 100-person hardware organization and $300 million of fleet deployments; Keene brings multi-market operations and business development. This is exceptional founder-market fit for distributed mobility infrastructure.

**Verdict — 7.3/10, Medium confidence.** The team has already scaled analogous infrastructure. The company must demonstrate a full reset cycle, multi-vehicle compatibility, station uptime, and an operator-backed pilot.

## 9. Advanced Metal Research

**Money.** Advanced Metal Research is an active YC Spring 2026 accelerator/seed company, implying YC's standard $500,000 investment. No outside round, customer, purchase order, deployment, revenue, or weld-quality benchmark is public. [YC company profile and Spring 2026 batch entry, accessed June 20, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/advanced-metal-research)

**Technology — disclosed.** The company is building American-made robotic welding cells that combine computer vision for real-time seam tracking with post-weld inspection. Its stated objective is a closed-loop manufacturing system that learns from every weld rather than a fixed robot replaying a programmed path. Public sources do not disclose welding process, robot vendor, sensor stack, model architecture, supported materials, part geometries, cycle time, or defect accuracy. [Company site, accessed June 20, 2026](https://advancedmetalresearch.com/)

**Technology — inference and gaps.** A likely cell uses CAD or an initial scan to plan the path, then laser profilometry or stereo/structured-light vision to locate the joint and correct torch pose during welding. Cameras or photodiodes may observe the arc and melt pool; current, voltage, wire-feed, travel speed, gas flow, and thermal signals provide process context. Post-weld inspection can identify bead geometry and surface defects, while destructive tests or nondestructive evaluation generate labels for deeper quality prediction.

This is harder than ordinary vision. Arc saturation, smoke, spatter, reflective metal, heat distortion, changing gap geometry, and occlusion degrade sensing. A visually acceptable bead can still contain porosity, lack of fusion, or internal cracking. Closed-loop control must respond within milliseconds without destabilizing the weld, and any learned parameter update needs bounds because a model error can scrap an expensive assembly. Commodity robot arms, weld controllers, seam trackers, and vision models exist. Defensibility requires a multimodal weld dataset tied to verified quality, robust control software, application tooling, and fast commissioning across variable low-volume work.

**Founders.** Julian Fried previously worked as a welding foreman, supplying direct process knowledge. Stephen Lin built NASA mission-control systems used during 19 spacewalks, relevant to telemetry and high-reliability interfaces. Angus Muffatti brings ML optimization publications, rocket hardware, and prior startup execution. The mix is unusually complementary. Missing evidence is whether the team has industrial robot safety, welding-code certification, and field-service depth.

**Verdict — 7.0/10, Medium-low confidence.** The problem and team are strong, but the company currently exposes almost no product evidence. A customer weld trial with destructive/NDT validation would change the score materially.

## 10. ReasonBlocks

**Money.** ReasonBlocks has YC's $500,000 standard investment and no disclosed additional round. Its SDK and platform are publicly available, but no user count, paying customer, revenue, or production case study is disclosed. Evidence consists of an internal benchmark on 75 SWE-bench Pro tasks using Sonnet 4.6. [YC launch, April 27, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/launches/Q5O-reasonblocks-stop-your-ai-agents-from-burning-money-re-learning-what-they-already-know)

**Technology — disclosed.** The runtime inserts six monitors into agent calls to detect loops, redundant work, and second-guessing; compresses stale tool outputs and redundant context; and extracts successful patterns into a private reasoning library that can be matched and injected into later runs. The company reports 52% fewer tokens, 42% higher accuracy, and 70% fewer budget-cap hits on its 75-problem evaluation. It says the integration is model- and framework-independent.

**Technology — inference and gaps.** Runtime monitors likely consume traces containing prompts, model outputs, tool calls, state transitions, errors, and token budgets. Rules or classifiers identify repeated actions and stalled state; compression summarizes or deletes low-value history; retrieval finds prior trajectories with similar task features. The hard part is intervention precision. A repeated tool call may be a necessary retry, "stale" context may contain the decisive constraint, and a previously successful pattern may be wrong in a new environment. Bad lessons can compound just as good ones can.

The benchmark is too small and underspecified to establish generality. "42% accuracy gain" could mean relative improvement or percentage points; task selection, baseline prompts, model variance, retries, and confidence intervals are not published. SWE tasks also reward code-centric behavior and may not transfer to regulated or transactional agents. Commodity context compression, trace stores, memory layers, and guardrail hooks are widely available. A moat would require a high-precision intervention engine, safe provenance-aware memory, evaluation tooling, and production traces across many workflows. Privacy is material because the reasoning library may store customer data, credentials, tool output, or incorrect conclusions.

**Founders.** Sajeev Magesh and Rohan Vij have long collaboration history plus ML, scientific-computing, and energy research experience. They are technically promising but have limited disclosed production infrastructure or enterprise-security experience.

**Verdict — 6.8/10, Low-medium confidence.** The mechanism is more concrete than generic "agent memory," but the reported gain needs independent reproduction and ablations showing which monitor or compression step helps without hiding failures.
