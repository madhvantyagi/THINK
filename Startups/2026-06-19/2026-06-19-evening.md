## Search universe and method

This report screened approximately **105 companies** announced, funded, launched, or materially updated during roughly the last six months, with emphasis on the last four weeks. There are **no previous startup reports in this repository**, including no morning report for June 19, so all ten companies are first-time coverage. Selection favored companies where technology is central, the recent event is verifiable, and there is enough public evidence to analyze mechanisms rather than repeat a funding announcement. Exclusions included consumer wrappers with no disclosed technical leverage, companies whose only evidence was a syndicated press release, and stealth companies for which even a cautious architecture reconstruction would be mostly speculation.

The evidence quality is uneven by design: Coram, Katalyst, RadixArk, Sarvam, and Unframe disclose meaningful technical detail; Ent and Genesis disclose product behavior but not enough benchmark methodology; Bubble Robotics, StrainX, and HighGround are included because the underlying engineering problems are important, but their low disclosure is treated as a negative signal. “Inference” below means an analyst reconstruction from observable product behavior and standard system constraints, not a company claim.

## 1. RadixArk

### Snapshot

**Website:** [radixark.com](https://www.radixark.com/)  
**Location:** United States; exact headquarters not verified in reviewed primary sources  
**Founded:** Commercial spinout announced in 2026; SGLang originated inside LMSYS  
**Stage/investors:** $100M seed at a reported $400M post-money valuation, led by Accel and Spark Capital, with NVentures and strategic participants  
**Founders:** Ying Sheng, formerly at xAI, and Banghua Zhu  
**Category:** Open-source LLM inference, reinforcement-learning infrastructure, managed AI systems

### Problem, product, and buyer

Inference and post-training stacks repeatedly rebuild the same schedulers, cache managers, kernels, parallelism layers, rollout engines, and observability systems. The buyer is an AI lab, cloud, enterprise platform team, or model-serving company for which GPU utilization, time-to-first-token, inter-token latency, and rollout throughput directly determine gross margin and research velocity.

RadixArk commercializes infrastructure around **SGLang** for serving and **Miles** for reinforcement-learning post-training. The likely business model is managed infrastructure, enterprise support, performance engineering, and hosted tooling layered on open-source cores. Its wedge is not another model API; it is reducing the cost and operational complexity of running many models across heterogeneous accelerators.

### Technology

**Disclosed facts.** SGLang supports continuous batching, paged attention, chunked prefill, prefill/decode disaggregation, tensor/pipeline/expert/data parallelism, speculative decoding, quantization, structured output, multi-LoRA batching, and multiple accelerator families. The project states that it runs on more than 400,000 GPUs and serves trillions of tokens daily; GitHub showed roughly 29,000 stars, 6,600 forks, 14,000 commits, and a June 13, 2026 release when accessed. Its defining mechanism, **RadixAttention**, stores reusable prompt prefixes in a radix tree so repeated system prompts, conversation prefixes, few-shot examples, and branching agent trajectories can reuse KV cache rather than recompute attention state. [SGLang repository, continuously updated; accessed June 19, 2026](https://github.com/sgl-project/sglang).

**Analyst reconstruction.** A production request enters an OpenAI-compatible endpoint, is tokenized and admitted by a scheduler, then matched against cached prefixes. The scheduler must jointly optimize cache hit rate, batch shape, memory pressure, fairness, and latency SLOs. Prefill may run on different workers from token decoding because prefill is compute-heavy while decode is memory-bandwidth-heavy. For mixture-of-experts models, the system additionally routes tokens across experts and nodes while minimizing all-to-all communication. At rack scale, network topology, KV-cache movement, expert imbalance, and straggler control matter as much as kernel speed.

**Hard engineering.** The difficult work is cross-layer: CUDA/ROCm kernels, memory allocators, distributed scheduling, failure recovery, quantized numerics, model-specific attention variants, and day-zero support for rapidly changing architectures. A benchmark win on one model/hardware/configuration is not a durable moat; maintaining reliable performance across hundreds of combinations may be.

**Commodity components.** API gateways, Kubernetes, basic autoscaling, model registries, and ordinary observability are available elsewhere. vLLM, TensorRT-LLM, TGI, DeepSpeed, Ray, and cloud-native serving stacks compete directly.

**Bottlenecks and unit costs.** GPU-hours dominate. Secondary drivers are high-bandwidth networking, idle capacity reserved for latency, KV-cache memory, model-loading time, engineering support, and egress. Prefix reuse improves cost only when workloads share prefixes; unrelated short prompts gain less. RL rollout economics depend on generation throughput, reward-model cost, synchronization, and how much stale-policy data the training algorithm tolerates.

**Reliability and security.** Serving infrastructure must isolate tenants, authenticate model access, prevent prompt or cache leakage, survive GPU/node failures, and degrade gracefully during overload. The open repository is inspectable, but the security posture and control plane of RadixArk’s managed product are not yet public.

### Evidence, competition, founders, and moat

The strongest evidence is code adoption rather than funding: a large active repository, broad hardware support, integrations with major RL frameworks, and claimed deployments by large infrastructure vendors. Those adoption claims are company/project assertions and should be verified through customer references and measured workloads.

The founders come from the LMSYS research and systems ecosystem, giving them unusual access to both frontier model behavior and serving bottlenecks. The moat is contributor mindshare, benchmark credibility, integration breadth, production incident learning, and the possibility that SGLang becomes a standard abstraction. The risk is value capture: open-source users can self-host, clouds can internalize improvements, and chip vendors can optimize competing stacks.

### Market and wedge

The bottom-up market is the portion of model spend that can be saved or managed through better serving and training infrastructure. If a customer spends $20M annually on accelerators, a credible 15% efficiency gain can support a seven-figure contract. Expansion runs from inference to RL, multimodal serving, cluster scheduling, managed deployments, and performance consulting.

### Critical questions

- What percentage of SGLang’s claimed deployment base pays RadixArk?
- Are benchmark gains reproduced under realistic mixed traffic, failures, long contexts, and latency SLOs?
- Does the company control the project’s governance and roadmap without alienating contributors?
- Can managed software capture margin when cloud and chip vendors bundle optimized serving?
- How does the platform prevent cross-tenant KV-cache leakage and recover distributed state after worker loss?

### Success and failure cases

**Success:** SGLang becomes a default serving and rollout layer; RadixArk sells a neutral managed control plane across NVIDIA, AMD, TPU, and emerging accelerators, making it the Databricks-like commercial layer around an open standard.

**Failure:** vLLM or vendor stacks remain dominant, SGLang performance advantages narrow, and customers use the open source while buying orchestration from clouds. RadixArk becomes an expert-services company with limited recurring leverage.

## 2. Coram AI

### Snapshot

**Website:** [coram.ai](https://www.coram.ai/)  
**Location:** Bay Area, California  
**Founded:** 2022  
**Stage/investors:** $35M Series B announced June 11, 2026; $66M total; co-led by Ansa Capital and Battery Ventures with UP.Partners, 8VC, and Mosaic Ventures  
**Founders:** Ashesh Jain and Peter Ondruska, with prior Lyft/Zoox engineering experience  
**Category:** Physical-security platform, edge computer vision, multimodal investigation agents

### Problem, product, and buyer

Most security-camera systems record evidence but require humans to search it after an event. Data is fragmented across video, door access, visitor logs, alarms, and emergency workflows. Coram sells to schools, manufacturers, churches, warehouses, healthcare sites, and enterprises that want searchable video, real-time detection, access control, and coordinated response without replacing every IP camera.

The wedge is an **AI NVR/edge appliance** compatible with existing cameras. The platform then expands into access control, emergency management, visitor management, firearm detection, natural-language alerts, and “Deep Investigation,” an agent that reconstructs events across cameras and structured logs.

### Technology

**Disclosed facts.** Coram says edge devices run models locally on NVIDIA GPUs, keeping sensitive video near where it is generated rather than sending all footage to the cloud. It supports existing IP cameras, correlates video with access and visitor data, and lets an investigation agent analyze months of activity across hundreds of cameras and locations. More than 1,500 locations use the system, according to the company’s June 11 funding announcement. [Coram funding post, June 11, 2026](https://www.coram.ai/post/coram-series-b-fundraise).

**Analyst reconstruction.** Camera streams likely terminate at a local NVR that performs decoding, frame sampling, detection/tracking, and embedding extraction. Metadata and selected clips can be indexed locally or synchronized to a cloud control plane. Natural-language search probably maps a query into structured predicates plus visual/text embeddings, retrieves candidate events, and then applies stronger vision-language models to rerank or summarize. Deep Investigation likely decomposes a question into time, camera, identity, object, and event searches; joins the results with badge/door/visitor records; and produces a cited timeline.

**Hard engineering.** The hard part is reliable perception over bad cameras, low light, occlusion, varied viewpoints, and rare safety events—not generating a fluent report. Multi-camera identity continuity, clock synchronization, calibration-free deployment, and low false-positive firearm detection are difficult. Edge fleet management adds model rollout, rollback, telemetry, hardware failure, and storage-life-cycle problems.

**Commodity components.** Object detectors, face embeddings, vector databases, LLM summarization, ONVIF/RTSP ingestion, and NVIDIA edge hardware are widely available. Differentiation must come from data quality, fleet reliability, cross-system joins, evaluation, and workflow integration.

**Performance and cost.** Costs scale with camera count, resolution, retention period, local GPU capacity, storage, cloud metadata, support, and alert-review burden. Running all analysis at full frame rate is expensive; practical systems use cascades—cheap motion/object filters first, expensive models on selected segments. Latency matters for weapons and falls; investigation can tolerate minutes.

**Security and privacy.** Local video processing is a strong architectural choice, but customers still need encryption, signed updates, tenant isolation, audit logs, role-based access, retention controls, biometric governance, and proof that cloud features do not silently expand data exposure. School and workplace deployments create substantial consent and civil-liberties risk.

### Evidence, competition, founders, and moat

Coram provides named customer examples and says it operates at 1,500 locations. The architecture claim—edge NVIDIA inference with existing cameras—is concrete. Missing evidence includes independently measured detection precision/recall, false alarms per camera-day, investigation accuracy, edge hardware failure rates, and gross retention.

Competitors include Verkada, Rhombus, Eagle Eye Networks, Avigilon/Motorola, Spot AI, Ambient.ai, Flock Safety, and incumbent VMS providers. Coram’s moat can come from existing-camera compatibility, integrated workflows, video-derived labeled data, and the operational cost of replacing an embedded security system. Incumbents can add foundation-model interfaces, so search alone is not defensible.

### Market and wedge

The initial wedge is replacing an NVR or adding AI to existing camera estates. Expansion revenue can include cameras, storage, access control, emergency workflows, and per-site agent modules. The market is large because physical-security budgets already exist; adoption does not require inventing a new budget category. Procurement, privacy reviews, and local regulation slow sales.

### Critical questions

- What are measured false positives and false negatives for firearms, falls, tailgating, and custom alerts?
- Which video and embeddings leave the site, under what conditions, and for how long?
- Can Deep Investigation distinguish missing evidence from negative evidence?
- What is hardware gross margin and replacement frequency?
- How much customer growth comes from software expansion versus subsidized appliances?

### Success and failure cases

**Success:** Coram becomes the operating layer joining cameras, access, alarms, and response; edge inference creates privacy and bandwidth advantages; high switching costs and proprietary event data compound.

**Failure:** Detection performance varies by site, alert fatigue erodes trust, privacy restrictions limit features, and larger camera/security vendors bundle equivalent search and agents into existing contracts.

## 3. Katalyst Space Technologies

### Snapshot

**Website:** [katalystspace.com](https://www.katalystspace.com/)  
**Location:** Flagstaff, Arizona  
**Founded:** Founding year and founder list were not verified in the reviewed primary material  
**Stage/investors:** $12M financing reported June 18, 2026, led by Geodesic Capital with Fortitude Ventures  
**Category:** Autonomous rendezvous, robotic satellite servicing, on-orbit upgrades

### Problem, product, and buyer

Satellites are expensive, slow to replace, and usually cannot be repaired, refueled, upgraded, or moved after launch. Katalyst’s immediate proof point is **LINK**, a servicing spacecraft intended to rendezvous with NASA’s aging Swift observatory and raise its decaying orbit. Its broader **NEXUS** vehicle is designed to transfer between orbits, approach and dock with satellites, carry payloads, and manipulate targets not built for servicing.

Buyers are NASA, the U.S. Space Force, intelligence customers, and commercial satellite operators. Revenue likely combines spacecraft sales, mission services, payload integration, and long-duration operations.

### Technology

**Disclosed facts.** NEXUS includes inertial and relative guidance/navigation/control, sensors and optics for rendezvous and proximity operations, collapsible solar arrays, propulsion, two payload bays, and a patented **Split Stewart Platform** robotic arm intended to grapple structures that lack docking interfaces. Katalyst claims technology-readiness levels from 5 to 9 across subsystems and says it tests sensors, actuators, propulsion, robotics, and software together rather than relying only on simulation. [Katalyst product page, accessed June 19, 2026](https://www.katalystspace.com/).

**Analyst reconstruction.** LINK must estimate both its orbit and Swift’s relative pose, plan fuel-efficient burns, approach through safety gates, and abort autonomously if navigation covariance or target motion exceeds limits. Close-range perception likely fuses star trackers, inertial measurement, GPS when available, optical cameras, and possibly lidar. Relative navigation must handle changing illumination, glare, Earth limb, target self-shadowing, uncertain geometry, and communication delay. Contact dynamics are especially risky because Swift was not designed for docking.

The Split Stewart Platform likely adapts parallel-kinematic principles: multiple actuated struts provide stiffness and controlled six-degree-of-freedom motion in a compact envelope. Compared with a long serial arm, this can reduce flex and simplify load paths, but may constrain reach and collision avoidance.

**Hard engineering.** The moat is not a generic satellite bus. It is safe autonomous RPO, unprepared-target capture, contact dynamics, fault detection/isolation/recovery, flight-qualified robotics, and mission assurance under strict mass/power/delta-V limits. Every maneuver consumes a finite propellant budget; excessive conservatism can fail the mission as surely as aggression.

**Commodity components.** Star trackers, reaction wheels, flight computers, solar arrays, and standard buses are purchasable. Mission-specific integration, navigation software, robotic capture, verification, and regulatory/insurance acceptance are not commodity.

**Reliability, security, and cost.** Unit cost is driven by launch, propulsion, radiation-tolerant components, qualification testing, ground operations, insurance, and custom integration. Security includes authenticated commanding, anti-jam communications, safe modes, and prevention of unauthorized proximity operations. A servicing vehicle is also dual-use counterspace technology, increasing export-control and policy burden.

### Evidence, competition, founders, and moat

The imminent Swift mission is unusually strong validation because mission success is binary and observable. Katalyst says it passed testing milestones, and NASA’s involvement validates a real customer need, though it does not prove docking success. Competitors include Starfish Space, Astroscale, Northrop Grumman’s servicing systems, Orbit Fab, ClearSpace, and government programs.

Founder backgrounds were not sufficiently documented in the reviewed sources, reducing confidence in team assessment. The company’s defensibility would rise sharply after successful unprepared-target contact: flight heritage, mission data, insurer trust, customer certification, and capture know-how are difficult to replicate.

### Market and wedge

The wedge is high-value rescue or life extension where a servicing mission costs far less than replacement and lost service. Expansion includes inspection, relocation, refueling interfaces, payload installation, defense missions, and end-of-life disposal. Market growth depends on launch cadence, satellite values, servicing standardization, and whether operators design future spacecraft for docking.

### Critical questions

- What are LINK’s capture mechanism, propellant margin, navigation sensors, and abort envelopes?
- How many NEXUS components have actually flown?
- Can economics work for ordinary commercial satellites, not just irreplaceable government assets?
- Who bears liability if contact damages the target or creates debris?
- Does the Split Stewart Platform have adequate reach and tolerance for uncertain grapple geometry?

### Success and failure cases

**Success:** LINK safely boosts Swift; Katalyst gains flight heritage and wins repeat government and commercial missions, then standardizes a multi-mission servicing bus.

**Failure:** The mission misses its rendezvous window, navigation or contact fails, or launch delay allows Swift’s orbit to decay beyond recovery. Even partial failure could freeze customer adoption for years.

## 4. Sarvam AI

### Snapshot

**Website:** [sarvam.ai](https://www.sarvam.ai/)  
**Location:** Bengaluru, India  
**Founded:** 2023  
**Stage/investors:** $234M round announced June 15, 2026, led by HCLTech and Bessemer Venture Partners; reported $1.5B valuation  
**Founders:** Vivek Raghavan and Pratyush Kumar  
**Category:** Indic foundation models, speech, vision, enterprise agents, sovereign AI

### Problem, product, and buyer

Global foundation models underperform in many Indian languages, code-switching patterns, accents, scripts, low-resource domains, and India-specific enterprise workflows. The commercial problem is broader than translation: banks, government agencies, telecoms, and consumer platforms need voice and text systems that work at Indian price points, latency constraints, and data-residency requirements.

Sarvam offers language models, speech recognition/synthesis, translation, APIs, enterprise deployments, and an Indus assistant. Its wedge is Indian-language quality and sovereignty; expansion is the full stack from models to applications and infrastructure.

### Technology

**Disclosed facts.** Sarvam released open-weight **30B and 105B** models in early 2026. Public model descriptions identify mixture-of-experts architectures, with only a subset of parameters active per token, and long-context support on the larger model. The company also offers speech and vision systems and emphasizes Indian-language training. Model weights and cards are available through its Hugging Face organization. [Sarvam Hugging Face, accessed June 19, 2026](https://huggingface.co/sarvamai).

**Analyst reconstruction.** The core stack likely includes multilingual data acquisition and deduplication, script normalization, transliteration and code-mix handling, a tokenizer tuned to Indic scripts, large-scale pretraining, supervised instruction tuning, preference optimization, safety tuning, and language-specific evaluation. MoE routing lowers active compute per token relative to dense parameter count but introduces expert balancing, communication, and serving complexity. Speech requires separate acoustic/front-end processing, language identification, streaming decoding, and possibly shared text representations for translation and synthesis.

**Hard engineering.** Data quality is the main scientific asset. High-quality Indic text is scarcer and noisier than English web text, with OCR corruption, inconsistent Unicode, transliteration, dialect variation, and duplicated translations. Speech adds accents, noise, code switching, and scarce labeled audio. Efficient serving of a 105B MoE model inside India requires reliable high-bandwidth clusters and careful quantization/routing.

**Commodity components.** Transformer/MoE architectures, open training frameworks, synthetic-data pipelines, vector retrieval, and general agent orchestration are widely available. The differentiation must be measurable language quality, proprietary data rights, enterprise integration, and low serving cost—not the word “sovereign.”

**Performance, security, and cost.** Training cost includes accelerators, networking, storage, curation, and repeated post-training. Inference cost depends on active parameters, context length, KV cache, speech duration, and concurrency. Enterprise and government buyers require India-based hosting, isolation, auditability, red-team results, and controls against prompt injection and data leakage.

### Evidence, competition, founders, and moat

Open weights allow independent inspection and create developer distribution. The $234M round and HCLTech involvement suggest enterprise go-to-market leverage, but funding is not model validation. Missing evidence includes standardized per-language evaluation, human preference tests by dialect, hallucination rates, safety performance, inference economics, paid usage, and retention.

Competitors include global labs, Google, Microsoft, Meta/Llama fine-tuners, Krutrim, AI4Bharat ecosystem projects, BharatGen, CoRover, and many application-layer voice companies. Sarvam’s moat could be language data, speech feedback, government relationships, HCLTech distribution, and a developer ecosystem. Open-weight release weakens model exclusivity but can strengthen adoption.

The founders have deep experience in Indian digital infrastructure and AI research, a meaningful advantage for regulated deployment and language-specific systems. The risk is organizational overreach across foundation models, speech, vision, apps, devices, and services.

### Market and wedge

The bottom-up wedge is replacing call-center, translation, document, and voice workflows priced per minute, interaction, or employee. Expansion into government and large enterprise can be substantial, but procurement is slow and gross margins may be compressed by custom deployment and compute.

### Critical questions

- How do the models perform by language, dialect, code-mix rate, and domain against fine-tuned global models?
- What data is licensed, proprietary, synthetic, or public?
- What is the cost per useful voice interaction at production latency?
- Does HCLTech create distribution or turn Sarvam into a subcontracted technology layer?
- Can the company focus enough to improve core models while supporting many products?

### Success and failure cases

**Success:** Sarvam becomes the default model and speech layer for Indian enterprises and government, with language data and deployment feedback creating a compounding quality/cost advantage.

**Failure:** Global open models improve multilingual performance faster, enterprise buyers choose cheaper fine-tuning, and Sarvam spends heavily on frontier training without enough proprietary usage or revenue.

## 5. Genesis AI

### Snapshot

**Website:** company launch was primarily documented through demonstrations and reporting; a stable primary technical site was not verified  
**Location:** San Carlos, California and Paris, France  
**Founded:** 2025  
**Stage/investors:** $105M raised from Eclipse, Khosla Ventures, and Eric Schmidt  
**Founder:** Zhou Xian; complete founder list not verified  
**Category:** General-purpose robotics, dexterous manipulation, robot foundation models

### Problem, product, and buyer

Industrial robots are reliable in fixed cells but expensive to reprogram and poor at open-ended manipulation. Humanoid robots preserve compatibility with human environments but add unstable, energy-intensive legs and safety complexity. Genesis’s **Eno** keeps human-like arms and hands while using a wheeled, height-adjustable body.

Initial buyers are manufacturers, logistics operators, and laboratories with repetitive but variable manipulation tasks. Genesis says targeted deployments and production should begin by the end of 2026, followed later by hospitals, services, and homes.

### Technology

**Disclosed facts.** Eno uses a wheeled base, folding three-panel body, two arms, and hands designed around human form. Genesis says its **GENE** model converts goals into steps and adapts to changes. The company develops the full stack: models, a 20-degree-of-freedom hand with 20 embedded motors, wireless data-collection gloves, an in-house simulator, and robot hardware. Reported demonstrations include piano playing, chopping, wiring, and one-handed egg cracking. Training used a few hundred human trajectories for cooking; most subtasks reportedly reached 90–95% success, while egg cracking and transferring chopped tomato were around 50–60%. The demonstrations were trained tasks, not zero-shot execution. [Business Insider, May 2026](https://www.businessinsider.com/genesis-ai-robot-cook-play-piano-human-level-performance-2026-5).

**Analyst reconstruction.** Human gloves capture joint motion plus force/tactile proxies. Those trajectories must be retargeted to robot kinematics, filtered, synchronized with video/proprioception, and combined with robot rollouts. A vision-language-action policy likely maps images, language goals, and robot state into action chunks or latent skills. A higher-level planner decomposes tasks while a lower-level controller handles grasping and motion. Simulation provides perturbations and regression testing, but real-to-sim and sim-to-real gaps remain.

**Hard engineering.** Dexterous contact is the core challenge: force control, slippage, occlusion, hand calibration, thermal limits from 20 motors, collision avoidance, and recovery after partial failure. Wheels reduce locomotion difficulty but cannot handle stairs, thresholds, clutter, or all human workspaces. Generality requires a large, diverse, correctly labeled manipulation dataset and reliable uncertainty detection.

## Cross-company conclusions

### Strongest opportunities

1. **RadixArk** has the clearest inspectable technology and the strongest developer distribution. The central risk is commercial capture, not whether the system exists.
2. **Coram** combines a real installed base, edge inference, and expansion into adjacent physical-security workflows. Independent error-rate and privacy evidence would materially improve the case.

- **StrainX** asks analysts to infer a platform before disclosing the molecule, organism, yield, or scale.
- **HighGround** assumes data normalization and workflow depth create a moat before incumbents add equivalent natural-language interfaces.
- **Unframe** assumes reusable context converts bespoke delivery into platform margins.

### Themes worth tracking

- **The scarce layer is moving below the model.** RadixArk monetizes scheduling, cache reuse, kernels, and RL rollouts; Unframe monetizes integration and governance; Ent and Coram monetize enforcement close to the edge.
- **Physical AI claims need operational metrics.** Task videos are weaker than intervention rates, cycle times, uptime, and field maintenance. Genesis, Bubble, and Katalyst should be evaluated on mission reliability, not visual novelty.
- **Edge processing is becoming a commercial differentiator.** Coram uses local NVIDIA inference for privacy and bandwidth; Ent likely needs local enforcement for latency; Bubble must process offshore because connectivity is constrained.
- **Sovereignty is distribution plus economics, not only local training.** Sarvam needs measurable language quality, cost, and deployment trust; national positioning alone is not a moat.
- **Sparse disclosure should reduce scores.** Large rounds for Ent and StrainX do not substitute for architecture, benchmarks, or customer evidence.

### Improvements established for future reports

Because this is the first report in the repository, there are no prior analytical mistakes to correct. This report establishes a stricter baseline for later runs:

- rank inspectable repositories, government missions, named deployments, and measured failures above funding prestige;
- include a fact/inference split in every technical section;

- use upcoming falsifiable milestones—Swift docking, Eno deployments, Ent false-block rates, Bubble endurance, StrainX pilot metrics—as checkpoints for future updates.

## Overall verdict

This set contains three companies with evidence that can already be technically interrogated—RadixArk, Coram, and Katalyst—three credible but not yet proven platform bets—Sarvam, Genesis, and Ent—and four companies where the problem is clearer than the disclosed implementation—Unframe, Bubble, StrainX, and HighGround. The most important distinction is not software versus hardware or AI versus non-AI. It is whether the company has exposed a mechanism and a falsifiable operating metric. Companies that do so deserve higher confidence even when their metrics reveal failure; companies that do not should not receive technical credit merely for attracting capital.
