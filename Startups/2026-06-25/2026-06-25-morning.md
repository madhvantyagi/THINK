## 1. Kinro

**Money.** Kinro is a YC Spring 2026 company, so the verified outside financing is YC's standard seed investment. Its unusually strong traction signal is operational, not capital: the company says it licensed in 24 of 30 target states, gained appointments from 10 of 15 carriers, and had agents autonomously bind nine policies within three weeks. The founders also report $1M of annual revenue from earlier workflow software sold to insurance brokers, which matters because it proves prior distribution into the same buyer group rather than just a new demo.

**Technology.** Kinro is building an autonomous insurance brokerage for small businesses buying products such as general liability, business owner policies, workers comp, and commercial auto. The user is likely a business owner or operator who enters basic business facts: industry, state, employee count, payroll, revenue, vehicles, locations, requested coverages, and loss history. The system then has to normalize that intake into carrier-specific underwriting questions, match the business to carrier appetite, request or generate quotes, compare terms, and produce a bindable recommendation. In the strongest version, the agent can complete the transaction without a human producer touching every field, while still leaving licensed staff to review exception cases, coverage gaps, regulated disclosures, and carrier rejections.

The hard engineering is not a generic chatbot. Insurance quoting depends on state licensing, appetite rules, class codes, exclusions, carrier portals, structured forms, attachments, and audit trails. Kinro's moat would come from carrier appointment coverage, state-by-state compliance workflows, and a constantly updated appetite/rating graph. Analyst inference: the product probably combines deterministic insurance rules, document extraction, browser/API automation for carrier systems, and LLM agents for messy intake and explanation. Commodity LLMs can read forms and draft emails, but they do not by themselves know whether a carrier will write a specific restaurant in a specific state. The differentiated layer is likely the operational data captured from every quote, bind, rejection, and manual override.

Key validation gaps remain. Kinro needs to show loss-ratio discipline, E&O controls, and whether autonomous binding stays accurate outside simple admitted-market policies. Still, the first autonomous bind claim is meaningful if real because it crosses from workflow support into regulated transaction execution.

**Founders.** Pierre worked on Gemini post-training for financial services at Google DeepMind and Meta. Parth worked on ML infrastructure at Zoox. Corentin brings actual insurance operating knowledge from an MGA/insurtech chief insurance officer role. That combination is stronger than most AI-brokerage teams: the technical founders understand production ML, and the insurance founder understands licensing, carriers, and risk.

**Verdict.** High-upside vertical AI because the product is tied to regulated execution, not just document summarization. Rating: 8.4/10. Confidence: medium-high.

## 2. Trellis

**Money.** Trellis is a YC Spring 2026 seed-stage company. Its disclosed traction is one of the strongest in the batch: the company reports $500K ARR, 35 customers, 3,000 listings, and roughly 15% week-over-week growth after two months. For an operations product, 3,000 managed listings is a more useful signal than demo counts because each listing creates daily guest, cleaning, maintenance, and owner workflows.

**Technology.** Trellis builds AI agents for short-term-rental operators. The user is a property manager or hospitality operations team. Inputs enter from property-management systems, Airbnb/VRBO messages, WhatsApp, email, cleaner schedules, maintenance requests, owner questions, guest claims, vendor invoices, and listing context. The system maintains an operational state for each property and reservation: who is checking in, what tasks are open, which vendor is responsible, what the house rules say, what the last guest reported, and what the manager already approved.

The agent then acts across tools. A guest asks why the TV is not working; Trellis checks the listing, prior maintenance notes, and guest history, sends a troubleshooting response, creates a task, dispatches a technician if needed, and updates the guest. If the damage is claimable, the system can assemble evidence and file an Airbnb claim. If a cleaner reports a missing supply, it can coordinate replacement without waiting for a manager. The human review point should be high-cost exceptions: refund decisions, claim disputes, vendor spend above limits, owner-sensitive messages, and safety incidents.

Architecturally, the hard part is durable workflow orchestration across 200+ tools, not the first chat reply. Trellis needs reliable integrations with PMS systems, OTAs, cleaning apps, messaging channels, calendars, and accounting tools. It also needs memory that is scoped correctly by property, guest, owner, reservation, and task. Analyst inference: the system likely uses event ingestion, tool-specific connectors, a property/reservation graph, retrieval over operational records, and LLM planners constrained by approval policies. Cost pressure is real because STR messaging volume is high and margins for smaller managers are limited. The automation-rate claim is credible only if Trellis proves that it can avoid compounding errors during turnovers and emergencies.

**Founders.** Lodo previously built AI customer-service agents for short-term rentals at Conduit and scaled a property-management company to about $2M revenue and 150 listings. Jan has STR sales and media/event experience. This is strong founder-market fit: they know the workflows, the buyer, and the painful edge cases.

**Verdict.** A strong vertical operations agent with early revenue and workflow density. Rating: 8.3/10. Confidence: medium-high.

Sources: [YC profile, Spring 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/trellistech); [Trellis website, accessed June 25, 2026](https://www.trellistech.com); [YC standard deal](https://www.ycombinator.com/deal).

## 3. Ontora

**Money.** Ontora is a YC Spring 2026 company and disclosed $700K raised before its official round. The customer signal is also useful: 5 enterprise design customers, 150+ inbound demos, and buyer examples including an 800-person agency, a 1,000-person roll-up, a PE firm, and a US facilities marketplace. This does not yet prove repeatable pricing, but it shows the problem is painful enough for larger organizations to test.

**Technology.** Ontora interviews employees and turns undocumented operations into a usable map of workflows, bottlenecks, and automation opportunities. The user is an executive, transformation lead, operations leader, or investor trying to understand how work actually happens inside a company. Inputs are employee interviews, follow-up questions, transcripts, uploaded documents, tool data, and possibly org-chart/context data. The system schedules or conducts many interviews in parallel, asks role-specific questions, detects missing details, follows up, and then synthesizes themes, process maps, handoff failures, and ROI-ranked automation candidates.

The product has a clear operational loop. First, a leader scopes the investigation: team, business process, acquisition diligence target, or automation objective. Second, Ontora interviews employees, usually through voice or structured chat. Third, the system links answers to departments, roles, tools, documents, and recurring handoffs. Fourth, it produces an executive artifact: process map, bottleneck list, risk register, automation roadmap, and maybe specific SOPs or agent candidates. Humans review by validating sensitive findings, correcting process descriptions, and deciding which recommendations are politically and operationally acceptable.

The technical challenge is synthesis under ambiguity. Employees describe the same workflow differently, omit unofficial workarounds, and contradict each other. Ontora therefore needs entity resolution, contradiction detection, confidence scoring, and provenance back to quotes or transcripts. Analyst inference: the system likely builds a workflow graph over actors, systems, inputs, outputs, frequency, exceptions, and business impact. LLMs help with interviews and summarization, but the moat comes from structured methodology, enterprise trust, and accumulated process benchmarks across industries. The biggest risk is that outputs become consulting-style slideware unless Ontora can tie findings to implementable changes and measurable ROI.

**Founders.** Leon has AI consulting experience with Siemens and Mercedes and has built distribution through a large conference and prior fundraising. Maximilian was an AI engineer at Porsche procurement. David worked on AI projects at Google and previously exited a YC company. This is a credible team for enterprise workflow discovery.

**Verdict.** Strong wedge if it becomes the system of record for operational reality, not just an interview summarizer. Rating: 8.1/10. Confidence: medium-high.

Sources: [YC profile, Spring 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/ontora); [Ontora website, accessed June 25, 2026](https://ontora.com); [YC standard deal](https://www.ycombinator.com/deal).

## 4. Andustry

**Money.** Andustry is a YC Spring 2026 seed-stage company. The most concrete proof point is a first customer saving $400K on a $1.2M CNC-machine purchase. Its website also claims 30% average purchase-price reduction, 2x faster sourcing, and specific recent outcomes in automotive castings, robotics components, and direct aluminum supply. Those are company claims, but they are tied to purchase outcomes rather than vague usage.

**Technology.** Andustry is an AI-native broker for industrial parts, machines, tools, and intermediate goods. The user is a manufacturer or industrial buyer that sends a request by email: drawings, STEP files, bills of material, material specs, tolerances, quantities, certificates, delivery constraints, country preferences, and target price. Andustry searches supplier sources and active networks across Europe, China, India, and the US, identifies possible suppliers, checks capability and capacity, and returns a verified shortlist with price, quality, lead time, and commercial standing. Humans still matter at supplier qualification, negotiation, final award, and quality disputes.

This is technically harder than a B2B lead database. Industrial sourcing has sparse public data, messy part descriptions, local supplier networks, certification requirements, freight constraints, tooling constraints, and cross-border payment/compliance issues. The system must parse engineering artifacts, map them to manufacturing processes, infer which suppliers can actually make the part, and avoid low-quality or non-responsive vendors. Analyst inference: Andustry likely uses document extraction, CAD/BOM parsing, supplier search over web/trade/import data, a structured supplier capability graph, and human-in-the-loop verification. The AI can expand the search space, but the real moat is verified supplier data plus buyer trust in the shortlist.

Cost and performance constraints are favorable if the company targets large purchases. Saving even 5%-10% on six- or seven-figure industrial spend can justify a high commission. The risk is service intensity. If every deal requires heavy manual supplier chasing, Andustry may look more like a tech-enabled broker than a scalable software company. The upside is that each completed transaction improves supplier coverage, part taxonomy, and pricing memory.

**Founders.** Het grew up around factories in an Indian industrial city and has CS/hardware optimization experience. Johann comes from an import-business family, studied mechanical engineering, did Cambridge/NASA/Airbus-related work, and later worked with airlines and defense at McKinsey. This is unusually relevant founder-market fit for industrial sourcing.

**Verdict.** Strong if it converts messy sourcing work into a compounding supplier-intelligence layer. Rating: 8.0/10. Confidence: medium-high.

Sources: [YC profile, Spring 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/andustry); [Andustry website, accessed June 25, 2026](https://www.andustry.com); [YC standard deal](https://www.ycombinator.com/deal).

## 5. HEVN

**Money.** HEVN is a YC Spring 2026 company. Its disclosed customer evidence is early but specific: the team says it brought in 12 customers from a prior banking launch. The founders also point to prior work at HERE Wallet/HOT Labs with a 15M-user wallet and $100M+ TVL security context. That is not direct HEVN revenue, but it is relevant proof that the team has shipped financial infrastructure.

**Technology.** HEVN provides global banking, contractor payout, bill-pay, card, account, and record workflows for teams and AI agents. The user can be a startup, remote team, finance operator, or an AI agent acting under a company's policies. Inputs include contractor emails, payee identities, invoice details, approval limits, currency/rail preferences, tax or KYC/KYB data, and spending policies. The system onboards the payee, collects documents, sets up named virtual accounts, routes payments over rails such as ACH, SEPA, SWIFT, UAEFTS, wires, cards, or USDC, and records the transaction for finance review.

The agent angle is important. HEVN's website frames the product as usable through app, API, CLI, and Claude Code-style workflows. A human or agent can request a payout, create a card, pay a bill, or reconcile records, but the product should enforce isolated subaccounts, spend limits, real-time monitoring, and manual approval for critical actions. The human review point is not optional; for finance infrastructure, approval policy is the product.

Architecturally, HEVN must sit between user workflows, bank/fintech partners, KYC vendors, ledger/accounting systems, payment rails, and possibly non-custodial wallet infrastructure. Analyst inference: the core system is a permissions and ledger layer wrapped around partner banking rails, with an agent-facing API that can initiate actions only inside predefined limits. Differentiation depends on whether HEVN can make agent-initiated financial actions auditable and safe enough for real companies. The risk is regulatory and partner dependency. HEVN states it is a financial-technology company, not a bank, so uptime, geographic coverage, and compliance quality depend partly on partner institutions.

**Founders.** The HERE Wallet/HOT Labs background is valuable because secure wallet and fintech distribution experience maps directly to HEVN's trust problem. The gap is proof of regulated operating depth at scale across countries.

**Verdict.** Interesting infrastructure for the coming agent economy, with real safety requirements and non-trivial compliance risk. Rating: 7.9/10. Confidence: medium.

## 6. RentAHuman

**Money.** RentAHuman is a YC Spring 2026 company. The company claims 500K users and $20K MRR within two weeks, plus media attention from outlets such as Forbes, Wired, and Futurism. Those are striking numbers, but the revenue is still early and could include novelty-driven usage. The more important signal is whether AI companies and robotics teams keep paying for recurring physical-world task execution.

**Technology.** RentAHuman is a marketplace where AI agents can hire humans for real-world tasks. The user is often not a person clicking a marketplace UI; it can be an AI agent or automation system that needs a physical action completed. Inputs are task descriptions, location, budget, required evidence, time window, safety constraints, and payment authorization. Humans then accept tasks such as recording chores for robotics data, checking in-store shelf conditions, shooting a local video, attending an event, buying supplies, or helping an AI-managed physical operation. Outputs are photos, videos, sensor-like observations, receipts, completed errands, or structured task reports.

The workflow is basically an API to human labor. The agent submits a task, RentAHuman decomposes or validates it, matches it to local workers, handles payment, collects evidence, and returns completion data to the requesting system. Human review is needed for task policy, worker disputes, fraud, unsafe instructions, and low-quality deliverables. The product's future value depends on making humans reliable components in AI workflows without pretending they are deterministic machines.

The hard engineering is marketplace orchestration plus evidence quality. RentAHuman needs identity checks, location verification, fraud detection, task routing, worker availability, payment rails, dispute resolution, and data-quality scoring. Analyst inference: for robotics/data-collection customers, the platform may also need metadata standards, camera instructions, annotation schemas, and quality audits. Commodity components include SMS/app notifications, payment APIs, and LLM task parsing. Differentiation would come from a dense labor network and API reliability for tasks that models cannot yet perform.

**Founders.** Public founder details in the screened YC profile were lighter than for the top entries, so the rating leans more on product novelty and traction claims than proven founder-market fit. That is a real gap.

**Verdict.** Big idea with strong timing, but execution risk is high because marketplaces, fraud control, and AI customer quality standards are all hard. Rating: 7.8/10. Confidence: medium.

## 7. Sidekick

**Money.** Sidekick is a YC Spring 2026 company. It does not disclose ARR in the screened materials, but it names industrial users such as Halverson Mfg, Ironwood Steel, Westfield Foods, Cascade Plastics, Monarch Pallet, and Riverton Tool. For a frontline-worker product, named manufacturing/customer logos are useful because the buyer is often skeptical of software that disrupts shop-floor routines.

**Technology.** Sidekick is an SMS assistant for frontline manufacturing, automotive, retail, and industrial workers. The user is a technician, line worker, supervisor, or new hire who does not want another app. Inputs are plain text messages, photos of equipment or defects, voice memos, and questions in any language. Managers upload SOPs, PDFs, Word docs, shared-drive files, videos, and walkthroughs. The system answers through SMS, cites the source SOP/page/revision, escalates unanswered issues to a manager, and turns resolved answers or workarounds into a searchable knowledge base.

The operational workflow is strong because it fits the environment. A worker texts "what do I do if this press alarm appears" with a photo. Sidekick retrieves the relevant SOP, identifies the equipment or error pattern, answers in the worker's language, and links the answer to the source. If confidence is low or the issue is safety-critical, it escalates. A supervisor can later see which questions repeat, which SOPs are unclear, and where training gaps exist.

The architecture likely includes document ingestion, OCR, video/transcript extraction, retrieval with citations, multilingual translation, SMS/MMS handling, role/site permissions, and escalation queues. Analyst inference: the key moat is not the base model; it is the operational data captured from frontline questions and manager-approved answers. Performance constraints include latency on weak phones, accuracy under noisy photos/voice, and avoiding hallucinated safety instructions. Sidekick needs hard guardrails around OSHA, lockout/tagout, chemical handling, machine faults, and incident reporting.

**Founders.** Justin So is a UPenn MS CS founder with prior finance experience at Arm. The founder profile is less domain-heavy than Andustry or Trellis, but the product design shows good empathy for frontline adoption: SMS first, no login, fast setup.

**Verdict.** Practical wedge into industrial knowledge management if citation quality and safety escalation are robust. Rating: 7.7/10. Confidence: medium.

**Money.** Salesgraph is a YC Spring 2026 company. It lists early partners including Mintlify, Tex, and Strala. The best evidence is founder-market pull: the team previously built an internal value-case generator at Mintlify that reportedly helped close millions in pipeline and higher-ACV deals. That is not independent ARR for Salesgraph, but it is a credible origin story for enterprise-sales workflow software.

**Technology.** Salesgraph builds proactive revenue agents around a dynamic context graph for enterprise deals. The user is an account executive, solutions engineer, sales manager, or founder selling complex B2B software. Inputs come from CRM, calendar, call recordings, sales notes, product docs, blogs, case studies, customer research, funding news, hiring signals, tech-stack changes, and integration docs. Before a call, Salesgraph sends a Slack brief with company research, likely business initiatives, discovery questions, relevant case studies, and technical context. After the call, it reads the transcript, detects unanswered gaps, drafts follow-up, updates the org map, proposes multithreading targets, and generates materials such as business cases, mutual action plans, demo decks, and ROI calculators.

The workflow is valuable because enterprise selling is context-heavy and easy to lose across tools. A good Salesgraph system is not simply summarizing Gong calls. It must maintain deal memory across stakeholders, map product capabilities to buyer pain, identify missing evidence, and surface the next action. Human review is needed before customer-facing follow-up, pricing claims, ROI numbers, and executive outreach.

Architecturally, Salesgraph likely uses connectors to Salesforce/HubSpot/Attio, call-recording tools, sales-engagement platforms, knowledge bases, and research providers. The central asset is a graph linking account, contacts, pains, objections, product capabilities, proof points, competitors, tasks, and next steps. Analyst inference: LLMs generate briefs and documents, while retrieval and graph constraints keep claims tied to approved sources. The risk is crowded competition: every sales platform is adding AI summaries. Salesgraph needs better account reasoning and action quality, not just nicer Slack messages.

**Founders.** The team has Mintlify, Microsoft, Amazon, seller, and engineering backgrounds. The Mintlify internal use case is especially relevant because technical enterprise sales often requires product-depth plus business-case writing.

**Verdict.** Strong workflow clarity, but category competition is intense and the moat must be the context graph plus integrations. Rating: 7.7/10. Confidence: medium.

## 9. primitive

**Money.** primitive is a YC Spring 2026 company. It has no customer count in the screened materials, but it has a live developer surface: public API documentation, SDKs, an MCP server, and paid tiers. For infrastructure companies, a complete developer workflow is a real readiness signal even before large revenue disclosure.

**Technology.** primitive builds email infrastructure for AI agents. The user is a developer building an agent that needs to communicate with people, vendors, tools, or other agents through email. Inputs are API calls or MCP tool calls: send an email, create an inbox, receive a reply, inspect metadata, handle bounces, or route an inbound message to a JavaScript handler. The system gives the agent managed `*.primitive.email` addresses, handles inbound/outbound email, and abstracts the painful infrastructure around MX, SPF, DKIM, DMARC, TLS reporting, bounce handling, IP reputation, OAuth, rate limits, idempotency, signed webhooks, and SDKs.

The operational value is clear. Email is still the universal business interface, but agents using normal user inboxes create deliverability, identity, auditing, and automation problems. primitive lets a developer give each agent or workflow a controlled mailbox, define handlers for incoming messages, and expose email as a tool inside an agent runtime. A human reviews by approving agent policies, inspecting logs, intervening in sensitive conversations, and managing domains or sending limits.

The engineering challenge is deliverability and trust. Sending email at scale is not just SMTP. primitive must maintain sender reputation, domain authentication, abuse prevention, retries, threading, inbound parsing, webhook reliability, attachment handling, and compliance around user data. Analyst inference: the strongest architecture is an email-control plane with per-agent identities, policy checks, event queues, webhook delivery, and observability. Commodity pieces include mail relays and SDK generation; the moat would be agent-specific abstractions, reliability, and reputation management.

The risk is that major platforms could add agent mailboxes. primitive needs to be faster and more developer-native than Gmail/Microsoft/SendGrid wrappers, especially through MCP and hosted tools.

**Founders.** The YC profile showed a four-person team but less public founder detail than some other entries. The product itself demonstrates good technical taste, but founder-market fit is less externally verified.

**Verdict.** Narrow but real infrastructure wedge; defensibility depends on deliverability quality and agent-specific workflow depth. Rating: 7.6/10. Confidence: medium-high.

## 10. Pavoot

**Money.** Pavoot is a YC Spring 2026 company. I did not find disclosed revenue or named customers in the screened materials, so its inclusion rests on a verified 2026 gate, clear workflow, and relevant founder signals. This is the weakest traction profile in the ranked set.

**Technology.** Pavoot is an AI event manager for in-person customer events. The user is a sales, marketing, community, or customer-success team running dinners, meetups, field events, conferences, or executive sessions. Inputs include target account lists, CRM data, attendee profiles, public company/person data, event goals, invite constraints, location, schedule, and follow-up rules. Before the event, the agent identifies high-conversion profiles, enriches records, writes personalized invitations, and tracks attendance. During the event, it organizes sessions, lunches, networking, or meetups and captures conversations. Afterward, it ranks leads, drafts personalized follow-ups, and syncs outcomes back into the CRM.

The workflow is useful because field events produce valuable but badly structured data. The best leads often come from informal conversations that never reach the CRM. Pavoot's system needs to connect pre-event intent, live attendee interactions, and post-event follow-through. Human review is needed for invite tone, VIP handling, private conversation notes, and final customer-facing follow-up.

Architecturally, Pavoot likely combines CRM connectors, enrichment sources, scheduling tools, email/calendar APIs, event check-in data, note capture, and LLM-generated personalization. Analyst inference: a strong implementation would maintain an event graph linking attendees, accounts, conversations, interests, sales stage, next action, and owner. Differentiation is possible if it captures data at the event itself, not only before/after. The risk is that generic CRM and marketing-automation platforms can copy the basic invite/follow-up flow. Pavoot must prove it can generate materially better pipeline from in-person events.
