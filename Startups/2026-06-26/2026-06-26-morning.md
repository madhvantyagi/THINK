## 1. Whitespace

**Money.** Whitespace is a YC Summer 2026 company, which verifies early-stage accelerator financing through YC's standard deal. It does not disclose ARR, contracts, or named customers. Its commercial evidence is product readiness: the website says businesses are usually live within 2-4 weeks, claims the product pays for itself within three months, and targets a narrow buyer with daily manual work.

**Technology.** Whitespace is built for wholesale distributors. A distributor connects ERP, CRM, inbox, spreadsheets, warehouse data, catalog, order history, and customer records. Incoming orders arrive by email, PDF, portal, or spreadsheet. The system reads the order, validates customer, SKU, price, stock, delivery terms, and catalogue fit, posts a draft or final sales order into the ERP, and routes exceptions to staff. For inventory planning, it uses sales history and seasonality to forecast demand and suggest reorder timing. For support, it answers stock checks, delivery dates, and order-status emails from ERP, WMS, and CRM records. Humans review exceptions, risky stock decisions, customer-sensitive replies, and any workflow the business has not yet delegated.

The technical core is a distributor-specific operating graph: customers, SKUs, substitutes, price lists, order cadence, margin, supplier lead time, inventory position, shipment status, and email context. Analyst inference: the product likely combines document/OCR parsing, ERP connectors, retrieval over business records, deterministic validation rules, LLM drafting, and feedback loops from staff corrections. The hardest part is not generating an email; it is avoiding silent order mistakes in old ERPs, handling partial stock, substitutions, customer-specific pricing, and bespoke integrations. Defensibility comes from corrected workflow data and deep integration into distributor operations. The main risk is services intensity if every customer has a unique ERP mess.

**Founders.** Alex Tung and Leon Yao both grew up around wholesale distribution businesses, and the site says they later built AI for large technology companies. That is useful founder-market fit: the buyer, data mess, and manual work are familiar rather than abstract.

**Verdict.** Strong vertical AI because the workflow has clear inputs, measurable labor savings, and high switching value if it becomes the distributor's operating layer. Rating: 8.2/10. Confidence: medium-high.

## 2. Coasty

**Money.** Coasty is a YC Summer 2026 company with no disclosed outside round beyond YC. Unlike most companies in this run, it has purchasable pricing: Starter at $19/month, Unlimited at $99/month, and API pricing such as $0.05 per `predict` call in its public context. Customer count, revenue, retention, and enterprise contracts are not disclosed.

**Technology.** Coasty is a computer-use agent platform. A user gives it a task such as fill a web form, update a CRM, send email, extract data, run a spreadsheet workflow, or test an app. The input can be a natural-language instruction plus browser, desktop, terminal, file, or integration context. Coasty runs the task inside a sandboxed VM or local desktop app, watches the screen, clicks, types, calls APIs where available, and returns completed work, logs, screenshots, or structured actions. Humans review live streams, approve sensitive workflows, inspect outputs, and intervene when the agent cannot complete a step.

The architecture disclosed by Coasty is unusually broad: web app, native Mac and Windows apps, REST API, MCP server, scheduled runs, sandbox VMs, OAuth integrations, and a screen-action API. The product says it scored 82% on OSWorld across 369 real-world tasks; I treat this as a company claim until independently reproduced. Analyst inference: the hard engineering is state recovery. Real software produces popups, slow pages, focus changes, permission dialogs, unexpected layouts, and partial failures. Coasty's value depends on detecting drift mid-task, retrying safely, and producing audit trails rather than blindly following a brittle script. Commodity pieces include browser automation, VNC/VM infra, LLM vision, and OAuth connectors. The moat would be reliability data, task traces, integration breadth, and developer adoption through MCP/API.

**Founders.** Prateek Jannu and Nitish Kovuru have public technical profiles but limited externally verifiable operating history in the YC page. The product itself is technically concrete, which partially offsets that gap.

**Verdict.** A real infrastructure wedge in computer use, but benchmark and reliability claims need outside validation. Rating: 8.1/10. Confidence: medium.

## 3. Insurf

**Money.** Insurf is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, revenue, recovered-dollar amount, or signed customers. It is asking specialty practices for a 90-day Access Recovery pilot and says it replies within one business day. That is pilot-stage evidence, not production scale.

**Technology.** Insurf builds a health-insurance decision layer around two products. Inveto handles denied-care appeals. A specialty practice supplies a denial, clinical chart, prior authorization record, payer policy context, and physician attestation. The system reads the denial, maps the clinical evidence to policy language, drafts a source-cited appeal packet, and tracks whether the payer overturns or upholds the decision. Humans review medical accuracy, attest the appeal, and decide what gets sent. Surely is a plan-cost engine: an individual or broker enters drugs, doctors, expected care, network needs, and plan options; the system estimates true 12-month cost across premiums, deductibles, copays, coinsurance, drug tiers, networks, and out-of-pocket maximums.

The deep asset is the "coverage-decision graph": each plan choice, denial, appeal, outcome, payer rule, clinical fact, and cost estimate becomes a structured row. Analyst inference: Insurf likely needs document extraction, clinical entity normalization, payer-policy retrieval, source-cited generation, workflow tracking, PHI controls, physician signoff, and outcome labeling. The hard problem is correctness under regulation: hallucinated policy citations, missing chart facts, or weak attestation could harm patients and practices. The upside is strong if every appeal outcome improves the graph used for future plan pricing and payer-behavior prediction.

**Founders.** Bryan Chung and Chang Lu are listed as founders; public YC bios are sparse. The product insight is strong, but operating depth in revenue cycle, payer policy, HIPAA, and brokerage regulation remains a diligence item.

**Verdict.** High-value wedge if Insurf can convert appeals into rights-cleared payer-behavior data. Rating: 8.0/10. Confidence: medium.

Sources: [YC profile, accessed June 26, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/insurf); [Insurf website, accessed June 26, 2026](https://www.insurf.io/); [YC standard deal](https://www.ycombinator.com/deal).

## 4. FlowManual

**Money.** FlowManual is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing. It discloses no revenue, paid customers, or outside round. Its site offers a free plan with daily usage allowance and invites contractors to a 20-minute demo, so the commercial signal is public product availability rather than revenue.

**Technology.** FlowManual is an AI back office for construction contractors. A contractor uploads PDFs: bid packages, signed contracts, specs, drawings, vendor buyout quotes, invoices, RFIs, and submittals. The system reads each document page by page, extracts scope items, price references, deadlines, insurance requirements, exclusions, and cost-sensitive clauses, then compares related documents. It shows line-level changes between a bid and the GC contract, or between a vendor quote and invoice, and ranks findings by dollar impact. Estimators, project managers, and executives still read the contract and own the decision; FlowManual is a safety net that points them to the costly lines before signing or awarding.

The system has a practical security posture: files, database, and extracted text stay on the contractor's own server, with a 2-4 hour on-site install. Technically, the hard part is document alignment across messy construction PDFs, drawings, scans, addenda, change orders, and inconsistent trade language. Analyst inference: FlowManual likely combines OCR, layout-aware extraction, contract clause classification, semantic diffing, dollar-impact heuristics, and a project-level document graph. Commodity LLMs can summarize contracts, but line-level evidence, page anchors, and local deployment matter because mistakes have direct financial consequences.

**Founders.** David Shijoon Bae and Michael Lin are two engineers and Harvard dropouts, according to the YC profile. Public domain experience is thinner than the product specificity, so the team will need fast contractor feedback loops.

**Verdict.** Strong narrow workflow with clear money-at-risk and a buyer-friendly data model. Rating: 8.0/10. Confidence: medium-high.

## 5. Perceptron ML

**Money.** Perceptron ML is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, revenue, or named customers. It is initially focused on law firms, but the website frames the product more broadly for businesses that win by responding first.

**Technology.** Perceptron is an event-driven AI engine. A user defines events that matter to the business: a court filing, data breach, government notice, market-moving filing, new RFP, distressed property listing, or internal signal. Perceptron continuously monitors configured sources such as courts, news, government and market data, the open web, and the customer's own data. When a trigger fires, it drafts a response such as a law-firm campaign, investor offer, construction bid motion, or trader alert. A human approves or rejects every action, and only approved actions launch.

The product is strongest because the workflow is bounded: detect, decide, act, with human approval. Analyst inference: the architecture likely uses source-specific crawlers or APIs, change detection, entity matching, event classification, retrieval over prior campaigns, draft generation, CRM or messaging integrations, and audit logs. The hard part is precision and timeliness. If it misses the filing, the value disappears; if it fires on noise, users lose trust. For law firms, the system also needs jurisdiction coverage, deduplication, attorney advertising compliance, conflict checks, and approval trails. Commodity components include web monitoring and LLM drafting. The moat would be high-recall event feeds, customer-specific playbooks, and a history of approved responses and outcomes.

**Founders.** Michael Marcotte and Peyton Marcotte are brother cofounders. Public bios are limited, so founder-market fit is not yet well proven. The product concept is coherent, but legal workflow and compliance expertise need verification.

**Verdict.** Timely, useful automation if signal quality is high; dangerous if the trigger layer is noisy. Rating: 7.9/10. Confidence: medium.

## 6. Isengard Industries

**Money.** Isengard Industries is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing. It discloses no defense contract, procurement vehicle, manufacturing partner, production quantity, unit economics, or outside round. The visible traction signal is a 20-person team and a sharp thesis around allied domestic production of precision-strike and counter-UAS systems.

**Technology.** Isengard says it builds "sovereign production" for allied nations to manufacture precision-strike and counter-drone systems by the thousand on their own soil. The likely user is a defense ministry, allied military procurement organization, or domestic defense manufacturer. Inputs are mission requirements, production constraints, local supply-chain capabilities, regulatory boundaries, and system specifications. The system output is not just a drone; it is a reproducible production stack for defense hardware at lower cost than prime contractors. Human review is central: defense users must approve requirements, legal compliance, testing, deployment rules, and production changes.

Because public implementation details are intentionally minimal, the technical assessment must stay high level. Analyst inference: the hard work is design-for-manufacture, sourcing, modular avionics, testing, quality assurance, guidance/sensor integration, counter-UAS detection or defeat workflows, and secure production transfer. The company cannot win only by assembling commercial parts; defense systems need reliability, supply-chain control, export compliance, operator training, and field maintainability. The moat could come from repeatable manufacturing know-how and procurement trust. The risks are unusually high: regulation, safety, export control, political constraints, battlefield validation, and the ethical weight of autonomous or semi-autonomous strike systems.

**Founders.** Francisco Serra-Martins and James Tennant lead the company. Public YC bios are sparse, so defense procurement, manufacturing, and operational experience remain open diligence questions despite the team size.

**Verdict.** Ambitious and timely defense-manufacturing thesis, but confidence is capped by limited public validation and high regulatory risk. Rating: 7.8/10. Confidence: medium-low.

The website describes an invite-only private financial membership for startups and an operating account with cards, bill pay, treasury, concierge service, and extended FDIC protection across partner banks.

## 8. Markov

Its public website announces "the world's most advanced computer-use dataset" and exposes a data-sample path, but methodology, volume, and buyer commitments are not yet public.

**Technology.** Markov produces tasks and data to train computer-use AI agents. The customer is likely a model lab, agent company, or evaluation team that needs realistic desktop/browser tasks. Inputs are target workflows, applications, task descriptions, screen states, human or agent trajectories, success criteria, and possibly evaluation rubrics. Markov creates or sources tasks, captures demonstrations or RL environments, labels outcomes, and provides datasets that teach agents how to operate software. Humans review task realism, privacy, correctness, and whether a trajectory actually solves the task.

The moat would be high-fidelity task environments, proprietary workflows, and a reputation with model labs.

**Founders.** Harish Ashok and Dev Mandal are listed founders. Public bios are limited but the product is well aligned with current model-lab demand for better agent data.

**Verdict.** Strong picks-and-shovels opportunity for computer-use models if data quality is genuinely better than generic trajectories. Rating: 7.7/10. Confidence: medium.

## 9. Poth Labs

**Money.** Poth Labs is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing. It does not disclose paid customers, revenue, retention, or outside funding. Its site is demo and waitlist oriented, so the evidence is product narrative, not commercial validation.

**Technology.** Poth is an agentic research platform for customer feedback. A company connects feedback sources and operating data: survey text, support tickets, app reviews, employee schedules, inventory records, shipment data, CRM records, and product usage. The system reads open-ended feedback, forms hypotheses about why users behave or complain in a certain way, tests those hypotheses against other data, rejects weak explanations, and returns evidence-backed root causes and suggested actions. Humans review findings, decide which operational change to make, and correct wrong explanations.

The workflow is stronger than theme clustering. Poth's demo shows complaints about bathroom supplies, then tests hypotheses against schedules, cleaning logs, inventory, and supplier deliveries until it attributes the root cause to wholesaler delays. Analyst inference: the architecture likely includes connectors, entity/time alignment, hypothesis generation, statistical checks, retrieval with citations, and a decision report. The hard engineering is causal discipline. LLMs can invent plausible why-stories; Poth must prove that evidence actually supports the root cause and that confidence scores are calibrated. Defensibility could come from reusable investigation templates and feedback-to-outcome loops across customers.

**Founders.** Mojmir Horvath and Matthew Wong are listed founders. Public domain history is limited, so the rating leans on workflow clarity rather than proven founder distribution.

**Verdict.** Useful if it escapes generic survey summarization and becomes a disciplined root-cause engine. Rating: 7.6/10. Confidence: medium.

## 10. Parasma

**Money.** Parasma is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, revenue, customer, hardware sale, or compute benchmark. It has unusually strong publicity: its site links coverage from The Guardian, Nature, Scientific American, New Scientist, The Economist, and others around human brain cells learning to play Doom. Publicity is not commercial traction, but it does verify that the science demo exists in public discourse.

**Technology.** Parasma is trying to turn living human neurons into compute. The user today is likely the research team itself, and later could be labs or specialized compute customers. Inputs are cultured neuronal networks, sensor/electrode readings, stimulation patterns, reward signals, training objectives, and software tasks translated into biological feedback. The system stimulates the cells, records electrical activity, interprets responses as actions or predictions, updates the training protocol, and measures whether the culture improves on the task. Humans handle cell culture, experiment design, safety, interpretation, and every claim about capability.

This is the highest science risk in the report. Analyst inference: the stack requires wet-lab culture protocols, microelectrode arrays, closed-loop stimulation, signal processing, reinforcement-style training, experiment tracking, and careful controls against noise or overinterpretation. The hard problem is not a viral demo; it is proving reliability, repeatability, scaling, lifetime, energy advantage, and programmability. Commodity components include lab hardware, cell culture techniques, and control software. The moat would be algorithms that reliably train biological networks and datasets that map stimulation to behavior.

**Founders.** Sean Cole is the solo founder. Solo-founder risk is material because this spans neuroscience, wet lab, hardware, software, and fundraising-heavy deep tech.

**Verdict.** Technically fascinating with huge uncertainty; treat as a science option, not a near-term compute business yet. Rating: 7.5/10. Confidence: medium-low.
