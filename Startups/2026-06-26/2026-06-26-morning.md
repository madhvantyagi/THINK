## 1. Whitespace

**Money.** Whitespace is a YC Summer 2026 company, so the verified financing is YC's standard seed investment. It does not disclose ARR, contracts, or named customers. Its commercial evidence is product readiness: the website says businesses are usually live within 2-4 weeks, claims the product pays for itself within three months, and targets a narrow buyer with daily manual work.

**Technology.** Whitespace is built for wholesale distributors. A distributor connects ERP, CRM, inbox, spreadsheets, warehouse data, catalog, order history, and customer records. Incoming orders arrive by email, PDF, portal, or spreadsheet. The system reads the order, validates customer, SKU, price, stock, delivery terms, and catalog fit, posts a draft or final sales order into the ERP, and routes exceptions to staff. For inventory planning, it uses sales history and seasonality to forecast demand and suggest reorder timing. For support, it answers stock checks, delivery dates, and order-status emails from ERP, WMS, and CRM records. Humans review exceptions, risky stock decisions, customer-sensitive replies, and any workflow the business has not delegated.

The technical core is a distributor-specific operating graph: customers, SKUs, substitutes, price lists, order cadence, margin, supplier lead time, inventory position, shipment status, and email context. Analyst inference: the product likely combines document/OCR parsing, ERP connectors, retrieval over business records, deterministic validation rules, LLM drafting, and feedback loops from staff corrections. The hardest part is avoiding silent order mistakes in old ERPs, handling partial stock, substitutions, customer-specific pricing, and bespoke integrations. Defensibility comes from corrected workflow data and deep integration into distributor operations. The main risk is services intensity if every customer has a unique ERP mess.

**Founders.** Alex Tung and Leon Yao both grew up around wholesale distribution businesses, and the site says they later built AI for large technology companies. That is useful founder-market fit because the buyer, data mess, and manual work are familiar rather than abstract.

**Verdict.** Strong vertical AI because the workflow has clear inputs, measurable labor savings, and high switching value if it becomes the distributor's operating layer. Rating: 8.2/10. Confidence: medium-high.

## 2. Prescience

**Money.** Prescience is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing. It describes itself as a health-benefits platform for ambitious employers and claims it can save employers 20-50% on healthcare while offering richer benefits. Its site shows illustrative savings such as lower per-member-per-month cost and a growth-company case study-style savings panel, but it does not disclose signed employers, covered lives, revenue, insurer/TPA partners, underwriting structure, claims history, or regulatory licenses.

**Technology.** Prescience sells employer health benefits. An employer brings headcount, location, current PEO or carrier plan, claims/cost baseline, hiring goals, and employee benefit preferences. Prescience designs a plan that advertises $0 employee premium, effective $0 deductible, same-day care, 24/7 care companion, GLP-1 coverage, wearables, gene testing, HSA support, and proactive savings. The YC profile says Prescience trains AI systems to create, operate, insure, and deliver healthcare for employer plans, including a "Time Machine" system that steers patients toward lower-cost health trajectories. Humans review plan design, clinical pathways, care navigation, underwriting, compliance, and any patient-specific medical decision.

The hard part is joining benefits design, actuarial risk, care navigation, and member experience. Analyst inference: Prescience likely needs claims modeling, provider/network data, care-pathway recommendation, employer cohort risk analysis, member triage, benefits administration, and auditability around recommendations. The moat would be lower claims cost without worse outcomes, plus a member experience that helps employers recruit. The risk is severe: healthcare savings claims need longitudinal evidence, insurance regulation is complex, and bad steering could harm members or shift cost rather than reduce it.

**Founders.** Aditya Jain is an MD from Harvard Medical School. Rishab Jain has CS/neuroscience background from Harvard College. That founder pairing is relevant for a product trying to mix clinical workflow and machine learning.

**Verdict.** Big, concrete pain point with strong founder-market fit, but claims require much more proof than the public site provides. Rating: 8.0/10. Confidence: medium.

## 3. Shepherd

**Money.** Shepherd is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing. It does not disclose revenue, paid seats, retention, deployment count, or named customers. It does expose a Mac app download and a concrete integration list, so the current traction evidence is product availability rather than customer validation.

**Technology.** Shepherd is a unified company memory for people and AI agents. A team connects Slack, email, meetings, Notion, Google Drive, GitHub, Linear, Stripe, Codex, Claude Code, Cursor, and related tools. Shepherd runs in the background, ingests communications, calls, documents, tickets, commits, and customer notes, then indexes them into a memory layer. A user can ask what was promised to a customer, what a teammate built, what follow-up is open, or which customer ticket needs an update. The system can also ping the user first and expose memory to agents through MCP. Humans approve outbound actions, customer-visible updates, bug filing, and any workflow with sensitive information.

The architecture is a company-context graph with permissions. Analyst inference: Shepherd needs connectors, local client capture, entity extraction, meeting/transcript ingestion, code and ticket linking, event deduplication, access control, retrieval with source citations, and agent-action APIs. The hard problem is freshness and authority. A Slack comment, signed contract, Linear ticket, and merged PR may conflict; Shepherd must know which source wins and who is allowed to see it. Commodity search is easy. The moat would come from reliable cross-tool memory, proactive open-loop detection, and safe agent handoff with source-backed context.

**Founders.** Philip Meng is listed with Stanford EE/CS, published research, and prior roles at Animoca Brands, 645 Ventures, and Tim Horton's. Ishan Ramrakhiani has Stanford biomedical computation and Dartmouth research experience. Elijah Renner has Stanford CS and research at Stanford AIMI and Dartmouth. The technical background is strong; enterprise security proof remains a gap.

**Verdict.** Strong timing for agent-enabled teams, but it must prove permissioning and freshness under messy real company data. Rating: 8.1/10. Confidence: medium.

## 4. FlowManual

**Money.** FlowManual is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing. It discloses no revenue, paid customers, or outside round. Its site offers a free plan with daily usage allowance and invites contractors to a 20-minute demo, so the commercial signal is public product availability rather than revenue.

**Technology.** FlowManual is an AI back office for construction contractors. A contractor uploads PDFs: bid packages, signed contracts, specs, drawings, vendor buyout quotes, invoices, RFIs, and submittals. The system reads each document page by page, extracts scope items, price references, deadlines, insurance requirements, exclusions, and cost-sensitive clauses, then compares related documents. It shows line-level changes between a bid and the GC contract, or between a vendor quote and invoice, and ranks findings by dollar impact. Estimators, project managers, and executives still read the contract and own the decision; FlowManual points them to costly lines before signing or awarding.

The system has a practical security posture: files, database, and extracted text stay on the contractor's own server, with a 2-4 hour on-site install. Technically, the hard part is document alignment across messy construction PDFs, drawings, scans, addenda, change orders, and inconsistent trade language. Analyst inference: FlowManual likely combines OCR, layout-aware extraction, contract clause classification, semantic diffing, dollar-impact heuristics, and a project-level document graph. Commodity LLMs can summarize contracts, but line-level evidence, page anchors, and local deployment matter because mistakes have direct financial consequences.

**Founders.** David Shijoon Bae and Michael Lin are two engineers and Harvard dropouts, according to the YC profile. Public domain experience is thinner than the product specificity, so the team will need fast contractor feedback loops.

**Verdict.** Strong narrow workflow with clear money-at-risk and a buyer-friendly data model. Rating: 8.0/10. Confidence: medium-high.

## 6. Perceptron ML

**Money.** Perceptron ML is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, revenue, or named customers. It is initially focused on law firms, but the website frames the product more broadly for businesses that win by responding first.

**Technology.** Perceptron is an event-driven AI engine. A user defines events that matter to the business: a court filing, data breach, government notice, market-moving filing, new RFP, distressed property listing, or internal signal. Perceptron continuously monitors configured sources such as courts, news, government and market data, the open web, and the customer's own data. When a trigger fires, it drafts a response such as a law-firm campaign, investor offer, construction bid motion, or trader alert. A human approves or rejects every action, and only approved actions launch.

The product is strongest because the workflow is bounded: detect, decide, act, with human approval. Analyst inference: the architecture likely uses source-specific crawlers or APIs, change detection, entity matching, event classification, retrieval over prior campaigns, draft generation, CRM or messaging integrations, and audit logs. The hard part is precision and timeliness. If it misses the filing, the value disappears; if it fires on noise, users lose trust. For law firms, the system also needs jurisdiction coverage, deduplication, attorney advertising compliance, conflict checks, and approval trails. Commodity components include web monitoring and LLM drafting. The moat would be high-recall event feeds, customer-specific playbooks, and a history of approved responses and outcomes.

**Founders.** Michael Marcotte and Peyton Marcotte are brother cofounders. Public bios are limited, so founder-market fit is not yet well proven. The product concept is coherent, but legal workflow and compliance expertise need verification.

**Verdict.** Timely, useful automation if signal quality is high; dangerous if the trigger layer is noisy. Rating: 7.9/10. Confidence: medium.

## 7. Bloomy

**Money.** Bloomy is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing. It does not disclose paid schools, family subscriptions, revenue, learning-outcome studies, or district contracts. The product has a sign-in surface, demo request flow, and school/family positioning, but public validation is still early.

**Technology.** Bloomy is an AI-powered mastery-learning system for K-12, starting with English Language Arts. A student or school connects benchmark data such as i-Ready, NWEA MAP, Bloomy's own diagnostic, grade level, standards, and skill evidence. The system maps the student onto an ELA skill graph across reading, writing, vocabulary, grammar, mechanics, and related domains. It assigns the next lesson, gives guided practice, checks mastery, and only advances the student after a stated 90% proficiency threshold. BloomyBot acts as a Socratic tutor: when an answer is wrong, it diagnoses the misconception and prompts the student through reasoning rather than simply giving the answer. Teachers and parents review progress, override plans, and intervene when the student is stuck.

The architecture likely combines diagnostic scoring, standards mapping, adaptive sequencing, content generation, misconception classification, tutor dialogue controls, and dashboards. The hard engineering is pedagogical reliability. A tutor must not over-help during mastery assessment, skip foundational gaps, misread grade-level standards, or create unsafe student interactions. Analyst inference: Bloomy needs guardrails around hints, age-appropriate content, teacher visibility, and data privacy. The moat would come from a high-quality skill map, student response data, and proven learning gains. The risk is crowded edtech plus slow school procurement.

**Founders.** Alex Southmayd was a 7th-grade Teach For America teacher, GMAT/GRE instructor, Lyft New England driver-acquisition leader, McKinsey engagement manager, and Stanford GSB MBA. That gives unusually direct education plus go-to-market experience for a solo founder.

**Verdict.** Strong education workflow and credible founder context; it needs measured student outcomes and paid school adoption. Rating: 7.8/10. Confidence: medium.

## 9. Markov

**Technology.** Markov produces tasks and data to train computer-use AI agents. The customer is likely a model lab, agent company, or evaluation team that needs realistic desktop/browser tasks. Inputs are target workflows, applications, task descriptions, screen states, human or agent trajectories, success criteria, and evaluation rubrics. **Founders.** Harish Ashok and Dev Mandal are listed founders. Public bios are limited but the product is well aligned with current model-lab demand for better agent data.

**Verdict.** Strong picks-and-shovels opportunity for computer-use models if data quality is genuinely better than generic trajectories. Rating: 7.7/10. Confidence: medium.

## 10. Poth Labs

**Money.** Poth Labs is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing. It does not disclose paid customers, revenue, retention, or outside funding. Its site is demo and waitlist oriented, so the evidence is product narrative, not commercial validation.

**Technology.** Poth is an agentic research platform for customer feedback. A company connects feedback sources and operating data: survey text, support tickets, app reviews, employee schedules, inventory records, shipment data, CRM records, and product usage. The system reads open-ended feedback, forms hypotheses about why users behave or complain in a certain way, tests those hypotheses against other data, rejects weak explanations, and returns evidence-backed root causes and suggested actions. Humans review findings, decide which operational change to make, and correct wrong explanations.

The workflow is stronger than theme clustering. Poth's demo shows complaints about bathroom supplies, then tests hypotheses against schedules, cleaning logs, inventory, and supplier deliveries until it attributes the root cause to wholesaler delays. Analyst inference: the architecture likely includes connectors, entity/time alignment, hypothesis generation, statistical checks, retrieval with citations, and a decision report. The hard engineering is causal discipline. LLMs can invent plausible why-stories; Poth must prove that evidence actually supports the root cause and that confidence scores are calibrated. Defensibility could come from reusable investigation templates and feedback-to-outcome loops across customers.

**Founders.** Mojmir Horvath and Matthew Wong are listed founders. Public domain history is limited, so the rating leans on workflow clarity rather than proven founder distribution.

**Verdict.** Useful if it escapes generic survey summarization and becomes a disciplined root-cause engine. Rating: 7.6/10. Confidence: medium.
