## 1. Datost

**Money.** Datost has YC's standard seed investment and no disclosed outside round or revenue. Its site names Finch Legal, OneLeet, Moda, Clad, Juno, Memoir, Ploy, and other users, claims **18,402 queries answered in the current week**, and shows an average 42 seconds to first answer. The YC launch says an internal predecessor reached 80% usage at Traba. These are useful adoption signals, but paid status, active seats, retention, contract value, query correctness in production, and support cost are undisclosed. [YC profile and launch, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/datost) · [Company site, accessed June 23, 2026](https://datost.com/)

**Technology — operational flow.** A business connects Datost read-only to warehouses, operational databases, CRM, billing, observability, code, documentation, and Slack. A user asks a question in a Slack thread or configures a metric to watch. Datost first resolves company-specific terms such as "churn risk" against a semantic layer, explores schemas in a sandbox, writes and tests SQL, has a second read-only model review whether the query matches the definition, and returns a sourced answer, chart, dashboard, spreadsheet, PDF, or slide deck. For monitoring, it runs the query on a schedule, detects a threshold break, investigates dimensions and connected systems, proposes a cause and action, and posts the evidence for a person to accept, correct, or ignore.

**Technology — assessment.** The product's real asset is not text-to-SQL; it is a versioned map connecting business concepts to tables, code paths, prior answers, owners, and exceptions. Datost reports **75.2% on BIRD-Interact versus 33% for the underlying Claude Opus 4.6 model**, with 91% on analytical questions. That supports the semantic-layer thesis, but public benchmark reproduction, error categories, data leakage controls, latency distribution, and production accuracy are still needed.

The hard engineering is trustworthy ambiguity handling. Definitions change, warehouse tables lag, joins double-count, finance and product teams calculate the same metric differently, and a syntactically valid query can still be economically wrong. A robust system needs query plans, lineage to source fields, data-freshness checks, permission propagation, unit and denominator tests, confidence calibration, and an explicit refusal path. Proactive monitoring adds multiple-comparison risk: watching thousands of slices can create plausible but spurious "root causes." Statistical baselines, seasonality, experiment context, and human confirmation should constrain recommendations.

LLMs, SQL sandboxes, MCP connectors, and dashboards are commodity. Defensibility can come from accumulated semantic corrections, high-quality evaluation cases built from customer questions, cross-source lineage, and the workflow position inside Slack. Security is material because Datost reads sensitive commercial data; read-only access reduces blast radius but does not solve prompt injection in documents, model-provider data handling, tenant isolation, or accidental disclosure into broad channels.

**Founders.** Maceo Cardinale Kwik built the initial system while engineering at Traba and says most of the company adopted it. Jason Wang managed payment infrastructure processing more than $200 million annually at Traba. Their direct exposure to operational data questions and production systems is strong, though neither public profile shows long enterprise analytics sales or data-governance leadership.

**Verdict — 8.3/10, Medium-high confidence.** Datost has a clear workflow, named usage, a measurable benchmark, and a sensible technical wedge beyond generic chat-with-data. The decisive evidence is paid retention, audited production answer accuracy, false-alert rate, semantic-layer maintenance cost, and security review outcomes.

## 2. Aster

**Money.** Aster has YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, customer, revenue, research contract, or commercial product. Its evidence is technical: it published results and artifacts across NanoChat, ProteinGym, and the NanoGPT speedrun. Compute intensity is substantial; the NanoChat experiment used **30 Nvidia B200 GPUs for roughly 50 hours**, so progress currently consumes expensive frontier hardware rather than demonstrating favorable product economics. [YC profile and launch, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/asterlab) · [Aster research results, June 22, 2026](https://www.asterlab.ai/research/announcing_preliminary_results_from_our_autonomous_research_system)

**Technology — operational flow.** A researcher gives Aster a measurable research problem, codebase, datasets, evaluation function, and compute budget. A planner identifies hypotheses and decomposes them into experiments. Thousands of worker agents can modify code, run experiments, collect metrics, criticize results, and feed promising changes back into the planner. The system ranks evidence, allocates more compute to promising branches, and returns a candidate method plus code and experimental artifacts. A human still defines the problem and evaluation boundary, audits surprising discoveries, reruns the best result, and decides whether the result generalizes beyond the benchmark.

**Technology — assessment.** Aster reports a **0.9098 BPB** NanoChat result, slightly ahead of a 0.9109 comparison system, after parallel search. It also claims a ProteinGym record in 30 minutes and 57-times faster execution than single-agent systems. The architecture likely combines a hierarchical planner, isolated worktrees or containers, experiment queues, shared result memory, hypothesis deduplication, budget allocation, and automated patch-and-evaluate loops. The difficult systems problem is coordination: thousands of agents can waste compute rediscovering the same idea, contaminate one another's evaluations, or optimize benchmark artifacts rather than scientific understanding.

The scientific problem is harder. A benchmark supplies a clean scalar reward; open-ended research does not. The system must propose useful questions, identify confounders, design controls, detect invalid measurements, and know when evidence is insufficient. LLM judges cannot safely substitute for reproducible experiments. A credible platform needs immutable experiment manifests, environment pinning, multiple seeds, statistical tests, negative-result memory, provenance from hypothesis to result, and independent reproduction.

Foundation models, coding agents, schedulers, and rented GPUs are available. The moat would be efficient search policies, high-quality research memory, experiment-validity checks, and proprietary evidence that the system discovers transferable improvements per compute dollar. The current benchmark lead is narrow enough that variance, implementation choices, and compute budget matter. The central validation gap is whether Aster finds ideas that survive external replication and matter outside tightly scored ML benchmarks.

**Founders.** Emmett Bicker trained large-context coding models and worked in reinforcement learning at Magic before founding Aster in January 2026. That is directly relevant frontier-model experience. Public information on the second team member and on senior scientific-method, distributed-systems, or domain-science leadership is limited.

**Verdict — 8.2/10, Medium-high confidence.** Aster is unusually ambitious and has published concrete results quickly. The rating depends on independent reproduction, compute efficiency, protection against benchmark overfitting, and evidence that the system can formulate and validate genuinely open-ended research rather than parallelize search.

## 3. smol machines

**Money.** smol machines has YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, customer, revenue, hosted workload volume, or cloud pricing. Its open-source `smolvm` repository has about **3,900 GitHub stars, 180 forks, 827 commits, and 64 releases**, with version 1.2.2 released June 23. That is strong developer attention for a 2026 company, though stars do not prove production adoption or a monetizable hosted service. [YC profile, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/smol-machines) · [GitHub repository, accessed June 23, 2026](https://github.com/smol-machines/smolvm)

**Technology — operational flow.** A developer or coding agent selects an OCI image or local root filesystem, declares CPU, memory, network, volume, GPU, and credential rules, then runs it through the `smolvm` CLI. smol machines boots a separate Linux microVM on the local Mac or Linux host, in smol's cloud, or on self-hosted servers. The workload runs with its own kernel and restricted network; results, files, or a persistent disk return to the caller. A developer can package the configured state into one `.smolmachine` artifact and rerun it on another same-architecture host. Humans review permissions, images, output, and promotion to production.

**Technology — assessment.** The disclosed stack uses Apple's Hypervisor.framework on macOS or KVM on Linux, the `libkrun` virtual-machine monitor, a custom `libkrunfw` kernel, OCI images, virtio ballooning for elastic memory, and optional Vulkan-over-virtio GPU access. The company reports sub-200-to-300-millisecond boot times, no daemon, network disabled by default, hostname allowlists, and SSH-agent forwarding without copying private keys into the guest.

The engineering challenge is making a VM feel as convenient as a container while retaining hardware isolation. Image conversion, filesystem layering, deterministic packaging, startup time, networking, volume semantics, cross-host compatibility, GPU drivers, and observability all create edge cases. A `.smolmachine` is portable only across compatible host architectures, and the current limitations include TCP/UDP-only networking, directory-only mounts, macOS signing requirements, and host-dependent GPU support. Cloud deployment adds scheduling, snapshot storage, multitenancy, billing, noisy-neighbor control, and rapid patching of kernel or VMM vulnerabilities.

KVM, Hypervisor.framework, OCI, and libkrun are open components. Differentiation lies in the packaging format, developer experience, embedded SDK, local/cloud parity, safe defaults, and an ecosystem of reusable machine artifacts. The moat is not the hypervisor itself; it is whether teams and agent platforms standardize on smol's artifact and control plane. Validation should compare cold-start distribution, memory overhead, snapshot size, I/O performance, escape resistance, and operating cost against Firecracker, Kata, containers, and hosted sandbox vendors.

**Founders.** Solo founder BinBin He spent roughly four years on AWS ECS, Fargate, and Firecracker-related systems and was a founding engineer on ECS managed instances. That is exceptional direct fit. Solo-founder capacity, enterprise support, and operating a global cloud control plane are the main team gaps.

**Verdict — 8.1/10, High confidence.** smol machines exposes real implementation detail, working code, active releases, and a founder with unusually relevant infrastructure experience. Commercial confidence depends on production users, security audits, cloud economics, and whether the portable artifact becomes a standard rather than a useful niche tool.

## 4. OpenWork

**Money.** OpenWork has YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round or revenue. YC says the three-month-old project reached **150,000+ downloads**; its repository shows roughly **16,300 stars, 1,600 forks, 3,187 commits**, and an active June 17 release. The desktop app is free and MIT-licensed. Its published Team Starter price is free for five seats and then $10 per seat monthly, while enterprise adds SSO, bring-your-own inference, long-term support, and SLAs. Paid conversion and enterprise deployments remain undisclosed. [YC profile and launch, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/openwork) · [GitHub repository, accessed June 23, 2026](https://github.com/different-ai/openwork)

**Technology — operational flow.** A user installs the desktop app on macOS, Windows, or Linux, chooses one of 50-plus model providers or a local model, opens a workspace, and asks an agent to work with local files, browser tools, plugins, skills, or MCP servers. OpenWork starts a local agent host, streams the execution plan and tool events to the UI, pauses for permission decisions, and writes outputs back to the machine. A technical teammate can package skills, MCPs, plugins, and configuration into one link; a nontechnical teammate imports it and runs the same governed workflow. Optional cloud workers provide isolated remote execution.

**Technology — assessment.** OpenWork is a Tauri desktop shell and team-distribution layer around OpenCode. The repository discloses local host and client modes, an orchestrator that runs OpenCode and OpenWork services, SDK connections, server-sent event streaming, local templates, permission prompts, debug exports, and a skills/plugin manager. Local binding to `127.0.0.1`, hidden sensitive reasoning metadata, explicit remote sharing, and bring-your-own keys are sensible defaults.

The hard problem is secure composition. Skills and MCP servers are executable supply-chain dependencies, provider behavior differs, and a local agent can access valuable files and credentials. OpenWork needs signed packages, permission manifests, trust and revocation, sandbox boundaries, secrets brokering, audit logs, model-routing policies, and repeatable version pinning. Sharing an entire setup in one link is useful but can also distribute malicious or outdated capabilities quickly. Enterprise value depends on central administration without destroying the local-first advantage.

The desktop shell, OpenCode runtime, Tauri, and model APIs are reproducible. Defensibility could come from the extension marketplace, packaging format, enterprise policy plane, compatibility work, and open-source distribution. The main strategic risk is dependency on OpenCode and rapid bundling by model vendors. The main quality gap is outcome evidence: download volume is strong, but task success, weekly retention, paid team adoption, and security incidents are not public.

**Founders.** Public YC material names Ben Shafii but gives little prior operating history. The repository's scale and release velocity demonstrate engineering execution, but enterprise security, ecosystem governance, and go-to-market experience remain difficult to evaluate.

**Verdict — 8.0/10, High confidence.** OpenWork has real distribution, visible code, and a coherent local-first architecture. The score depends on retained usage, paid team adoption, extension security, and whether its governance layer becomes valuable enough to survive platform bundling.

## 6. Ornadyne

**Money.** Ornadyne has YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, defense contract, paid pilot, manufacturing agreement, production quantity, or revenue. Its public launch shows a flying prototype and claims a target of hour-class endurance and lower acoustic and visual detectability than conventional small drones. No measured range, endurance, payload, noise signature, unit cost, or field-test result is published. [YC profile and launch, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/ornadyne)

**Technology — operational flow.** A military or security operator defines a reconnaissance area and mission constraints, launches a bird-like flapping-wing aircraft, and receives stabilized camera or sensor data while the vehicle follows a route or autonomous search pattern. The onboard controller coordinates wing motion, attitude, navigation, communications, and power management. Software can classify obstacles or targets and adapt the path, but a human operator should authorize surveillance objectives, monitor health and link status, interpret intelligence, and recover or abort the aircraft.

**Technology — assessment.** Ornithopters trade the mechanical simplicity of a propeller for potentially quieter, more natural flight. A credible system needs lightweight wings and joints with high fatigue life, efficient actuators and transmission, aerodynamic control across wind and gusts, inertial and visual navigation, stabilized sensing, low-signature communications, and autonomous landing. Bird-like appearance may reduce casual human detection, but military counter-UAS systems also use radar, RF, thermal, acoustic, and optical classification. The aircraft's moving wings create distinctive kinematics that may themselves become a detection signature.

The hardest engineering is endurance and reliability. Flapping mechanisms experience cyclic loads, actuator losses, vibration, and control coupling that fixed-wing aircraft avoid. Payload and battery mass directly reduce flight time. A practical system needs graceful degradation, accurate state estimation under wing-induced vibration, low-probability-of-intercept communications, encrypted command links, and safe behavior when GPS or radio is denied. Swarming or autonomous tracking would add coordination and rules-of-engagement complexity.

Commodity components include cameras, autopilot compute, radios, batteries, and object-detection models. Ornadyne's defensibility would come from aerodynamic design, manufacturing methods, control data, low-noise actuation, and field-tested autonomy. The stealth thesis requires controlled comparisons against quadcopters and fixed-wing drones across acoustic, radar, thermal, RF, and human detection—not only resemblance to a bird.

**Founders.** Geourg Kivijian spent more than two years researching flapping-wing robotics, published through AIAA/IEEE, and worked on Mars Sample Return coordination at NASA JPL and lunar rovers at Astrolab. Armen Arakelyan engineered Starship components at SpaceX and led machining at USC Rocket Propulsion Laboratory. The technical founder-market fit is excellent; procurement, classified-program operations, and volume manufacturing are less proven.

**Verdict — 7.8/10, Medium confidence.** Ornadyne has differentiated hardware and unusually relevant founders. The score is capped by absent field metrics, uncertain multi-sensor stealth, mechanical reliability, procurement cycles, and no contract evidence.

## 7. AICE

**Money.** AICE has YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, defense contract, paid pilot, sea trial, production order, or revenue. The company says it pivoted from building-energy sensors and assembled its first modular underwater prototype within a week. That proves speed, not operational capability. The site only states that the system is manufactured in France and intended for monitoring and protection of critical infrastructure. [YC profile and launch, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/aice) · [Company site, accessed June 23, 2026](https://aicepower.com/)

**Technology — operational flow.** A naval, port, or infrastructure operator assigns an underwater area and mission—patrol, inspect, detect, classify, or carry a payload. Multiple modular unmanned underwater vehicles leave a dock or support vessel, navigate using inertial, depth, acoustic, and environmental sensors, share detections when communications allow, and return tracks or imagery to a command station. Operators review uncertain contacts, retask vehicles, approve escalation, and recover units for charging, data offload, and maintenance.

**Technology — assessment.** Underwater autonomy is constrained by physics. GPS and ordinary radio do not work below the surface; acoustic communication has low bandwidth, latency, multipath, and limited reliability. Vehicles therefore need long periods of local autonomy, accurate inertial/dead-reckoning navigation, Doppler velocity or terrain-aided correction, sonar processing, collision avoidance, pressure-tolerant packaging, leak detection, energy-aware planning, and robust recovery. Swarms add distributed task allocation and deconfliction while communication is intermittent.

Modularity is commercially attractive because propulsion, battery, sonar, communications, and payload sections can change by mission. It also creates hydrodynamic, sealing, connector, software-discovery, and certification problems. A cheap vehicle that cannot localize precisely or return reliably is not useful. Performance should be judged on endurance, navigation drift, detection probability, false alarms, acoustic signature, payload, depth, recovery rate, and maintenance hours. Electronic warfare and adversarial sonar environments create additional failure modes.

Sonars, pressure housings, navigation filters, batteries, and autonomy software are obtainable. AICE's moat could come from modular mechanical/electrical standards, mission data, swarm behaviors, manufacturing cost, and relationships with European defense buyers. The one-week prototype and prior unrelated product raise a focus question: the team must show that the pivot has progressed beyond assembled subsystems.

**Founders.** Gregoire Chomette worked at NASA on asteroid threats and holds an MIT AeroAstro master's degree. Rémi Bouteiller is a European robotics champion. The backgrounds support aerospace and robotics execution, but public evidence of naval systems, underwater acoustics, corrosion/pressure engineering, defense procurement, and production is limited.

**Verdict — 7.6/10, Medium confidence.** AICE targets a real shift toward distributed low-cost subsea systems, but public evidence is extremely early. Sea trials, endurance, navigation drift, sensor performance, recoverability, and customer-backed mission requirements are essential.

## 8. Ara

**Money.** Ara has YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, customer, revenue, merged-pull-request count, production repository, or retention. Its site shows a working product and recognizable-company logos under "trusted by builders from," but that wording does not establish corporate customers. Commercial evidence is therefore pre-validation. [YC profile and launch, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/ara) · [Company site, accessed June 23, 2026](https://ara.so/)

**Technology — operational flow.** An engineering signal—a user report, failed check, flaky test, or repository issue—enters Ara. The system scopes a GitHub issue, creates an isolated macOS, Linux, Windows, or browser environment, reproduces the failure and records baseline evidence, selects a user-provided coding model, implements a patch, runs tests and captures after-state logs or recordings, then opens a pull request with the diff and evidence. Engineers inspect the patch, request changes, and merge. Ara then updates the repository wiki, changelog, or durable rules so later runs inherit the lesson.

**Technology — assessment.** This workflow is better framed than a generic "autonomous engineer" because reproduction and verification are explicit stages. The architecture likely needs event ingestion from GitHub and monitoring tools, environment images, branch/worktree isolation, model routing, browser and terminal control, test orchestration, artifact storage, policy gates, and a repository memory layer. Durable transcripts and branch-backed state are important because multiple agents must not overwrite one another or hide how a change was produced.

The hard problem is proving the bug and the fix. Generated tests can merely encode the patch's assumptions, flaky checks can produce false confidence, and a visual recording may miss semantic regressions. Ara needs independently specified acceptance criteria, before/after reproduction, regression suites, static analysis, security scanning, permission boundaries, and cost-aware retries. Updating a wiki after every run can also accumulate incorrect rules; memory needs provenance, conflict handling, expiration, and human approval.

Coding models, cloud sandboxes, GitHub APIs, and test runners are commodity. Differentiation can emerge from issue detection, environment breadth, evidence quality, model routing, and the feedback loop from merged work into repository-specific operating rules. Competition from Codex, Claude Code, Cursor, Devin, and internal agent platforms is intense. The product must show higher accepted-PR rates and lower review burden, not simply more autonomous activity.

**Founders.** Adi Singh previously built a Rust-based IDE and worked in applied ML at Scale AI. Sven Myhre has production ML-infrastructure experience and chaired a large Norwegian student AI organization. Their IDE and ML backgrounds fit the problem, but production developer-tool operations and enterprise security experience are limited publicly.

**Verdict — 7.5/10, Medium confidence.** Ara's evidence-first software loop is technically coherent. The score depends on accepted PRs, independent test quality, regression rate, cost per merged change, repository-memory accuracy, and whether customers trust it with broad engineering access.

## 9. Uno Wallet

**Money.** Uno Wallet has YC seed financing and support from Harvard Innovation Labs, with no disclosed outside round or revenue. Its site reports **25,240 people on the waitlist** and describes the product as a private beta. No active wallet count, card connection, payment volume, issuer/network agreement, interchange economics, fraud loss, or retention is public. [YC profile, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/uno-wallet) · [Company site, accessed June 23, 2026](https://myunowallet.com/)

**Technology — operational flow.** A consumer securely connects existing credit cards to Uno and makes Uno the default mobile wallet. At tap-to-pay checkout, the wallet identifies the merchant and category, checks each connected card's reward rules and current benefits, selects the highest expected return, and routes the payment to that card. The user receives the normal payment result and can review which card was selected and why. The human must be able to override preferences for credit limits, benefits, financing, acceptance, or personal cash-flow reasons.

**Technology — assessment.** The recommendation engine needs a current rule model for base rewards, merchant-category codes, quarterly activations, spending caps, statement credits, targeted offers, foreign-transaction fees, and valuation of points. Merchant classification is frequently wrong or ambiguous, and "best" depends on user-defined point values, annual-fee benefits, cash flow, and whether a card is accepted. A deterministic rules engine may be more important than AI; machine learning could improve merchant normalization, offer extraction, and personalized value estimates.

The harder constraint is payment infrastructure. Replacing the default wallet requires operating-system and network access, tokenized card provisioning, device-bound credentials, near-field communication, issuer authentication, transaction routing, dispute handling, and compliance with PCI, network, and platform rules. The public site says Uno routes the tap but does not disclose the mechanism or partnerships. If the product uses a proxy credential or funding layer, authorization latency, declines, chargebacks, rewards eligibility, and consumer disclosures become central.

Card-benefit databases and recommendation apps are reproducible. The potential moat is actual payment-surface control, issuer/network integrations, transaction feedback, and trusted consumer distribution. Platform owners and card issuers can bundle recommendation logic. The waitlist is encouraging, but technical feasibility and regulatory structure remain more important than demand.

**Founders.** Mordi Shadpour brings Bloomberg and Harvard business experience. Saket Joshi was a member of technical staff at xAI/X, studied data science at Harvard, and taught reinforcement learning. The team covers product/finance interest and ML, but public profiles do not show payments-network, mobile secure-element, fraud, or compliance experience.

**Verdict — 7.3/10, Medium confidence.** Uno has a simple consumer promise and visible demand, but the differentiator is access to the payment rail, not card-selection intelligence. Proof requires live transaction volume, routing partnerships, reward accuracy, decline/fraud performance, and evidence that users retain Uno after novelty fades.

## 10. HeyClicky

**Money.** HeyClicky has YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, revenue, paid user count, retention, agent-run volume, or inference cost. Its YC launch says the product was four weeks old and had gone viral on Instagram, with use by children, founders, doctors, and creators, but provides no views, downloads, daily active users, or conversion figures. [YC profile and launch, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/heyclicky) · [Company site, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.heyclicky.com/)

**Technology — operational flow.** A Mac user sees a small assistant beside the cursor and asks a spoken question about the current screen. HeyClicky captures relevant screen context, transcribes the request, explains the visible interface, or starts a background agent. That agent can research products, connect to Gmail, Notion, and Calendar, turn a Figma design into a webpage, build a local Mac app, summarize a PDF, or prepare an email. The user watches progress, approves access or consequential actions, checks the output, and corrects or stops the agent.

**Technology — assessment.** The product combines continuous screen context, low-latency speech recognition, multimodal understanding, desktop accessibility APIs, local file and application control, browser automation, connectors, background sandboxing, and task memory. The character beside the cursor lowers activation friction, but "sees everything you see" creates an unusually sensitive privacy boundary. The system must decide what pixels, windows, notifications, passwords, health data, and private messages can leave the device.

Reliable instruction is harder than a demo. Helping inside After Effects or FL Studio requires identifying exact application state, version, selected object, timeline, and available controls. Background actions need explicit capability scopes, approval before sending email or changing files, reversible operations, credential isolation, and defenses against prompt injection in webpages and documents. A local app builder also needs code sandboxing and protection from generated binaries.

Speech models, vision-language models, browser agents, and MCP connectors are accessible. Differentiation could come from a low-friction consumer interface, on-device context selection, application-specific interaction data, and distribution through short-form video. The moat is weak if the underlying action stack is generic and platform vendors expose similar screen-aware assistants. Unit economics may also be difficult because persistent multimodal context and long background tasks consume expensive inference.

**Founders.** The public YC page does not expose founder biographies. The site attributes the product to Farza, but prior technical, security, consumer-distribution, and operating experience cannot be assessed from available primary evidence. This is a material confidence gap.

**Verdict — 7.1/10, Medium-low confidence.** HeyClicky has an intuitive interface and broad consumer appeal, but evidence is mostly launch attention. Retention, privacy architecture, action safety, cost per active user, and repeated completion of real tasks will determine whether it is a product or a viral interface demo.
