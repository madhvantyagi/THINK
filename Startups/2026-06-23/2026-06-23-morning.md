## 1. PLAN0 AI

**Money.** PLAN0 has YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round. The standout evidence is company-reported platform usage: **$20 billion of projects** already running through its construction cost system. That is stronger than a demo but still incomplete: no named developer, lender, contractor, contract value, active-seat count, renewal, implementation effort, or estimate-to-actual error is public. [YC profile and launch, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/plan0-ai)

**Technology — disclosed.** PLAN0 analyzes architectural plans with vision models, creates detailed construction cost estimates in minutes, and aggregates historical and real-time project data through industry partnerships. The longer ambition is a "Bloomberg of construction": cost benchmarks, market-factor decomposition, stress testing for lenders, material substitution analysis, and an agentic layer for cost optimization.

**Technology — assessment.** The product has to solve three separate problems: plan understanding, cost normalization, and forecasting. Plan understanding requires PDF/vector parsing, sheet classification, symbol recognition, scale detection, room/assembly segmentation, cross-sheet references, and takeoff logic by trade. Cost normalization then needs a taxonomy that maps drawings into assemblies, quantities, labor categories, subcontractor scopes, geography, procurement timing, and escalation assumptions. Forecasting is harder: the system must separate observable drivers such as steel, labor, location, building type, and schedule from project-specific choices and missing scope.

The difficult engineering is trustable variance. A developer does not only need a plausible cost number; they need why it moved, what scope was assumed, and which changes reduce risk. A credible architecture should retain source coordinates for every quantity, version plan revisions, preserve assumptions, and produce confidence intervals by assembly. Commodity components include multimodal extraction, RAG, and cost databases. The moat would be a proprietary project-cost corpus, reusable cost ontology, correction loops from estimates to actuals, and integration into lender/developer workflows. Validation gaps are estimate error by project type, revision handling, and whether the $20B figure represents active workflow dependence or top-of-funnel uploaded project value.

**Founders.** Arash Barati has 12 years in real estate private equity, development, and lending across more than $7 billion of projects. Shervin Barati was an Apple engineering manager, and Dimitris Pagonakis brings Point72, Citadel, and MIT data/analytics experience. This is strong founder-market fit across buyer knowledge, product engineering, and quantitative cost modeling.

**Verdict — 8.3/10, Medium-high confidence.** PLAN0 has the strongest combination of specific workflow, high-value buyer pain, and early usage evidence. The key proof is named customers, estimate error against actuals, deployment labor, and whether its cost database compounds across projects.

## 2. Silmaril

**Money.** Silmaril has YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round. It claims its defense has stopped **$28 million of damages** for customers and says it is working with leading productivity, analytics, and legal companies. Customer names, paid status, contract size, benchmark design, incident definitions, and retention are not public. [YC profile and launch, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/silmaril)

**Technology — disclosed.** Silmaril is a prompt-injection defense for AI-native applications and agents. It says customers can integrate the firewall into frameworks such as LangChain or LangGraph with a few lines of code. Rather than only classify hostile text, Silmaril claims to understand application context, probe customer applications with autonomous red-team agents, generate threat intelligence, and block harmful executions in real time. It claims 2x better blocking than current state-of-the-art defenses with 10x lower latency.

**Technology — assessment.** The right unit of security is an action path, not a prompt. A credible system must model tools, permissions, data classes, user roles, policies, and possible side effects, then decide whether an input-output-tool sequence can produce exfiltration, privilege escalation, unauthorized action, or integrity loss. Red-team agents can map application-specific attack chains; the runtime firewall then needs a low-latency policy/classifier layer with deterministic fallbacks for high-risk actions.

The hard problem is avoiding both false negatives and workflow-killing false positives. Prompt injection is contextual: a malicious instruction in a customer email is only dangerous if the agent can read secrets or call tools. Silmaril therefore needs an application graph, exploit replay, semantic policy constraints, and production telemetry showing attack outcome, not only input toxicity. Latency matters because the defense sits inline with agent execution. Commodity pieces include jailbreak datasets, classifiers, and SDK wrappers. Defensibility could come from verified exploit corpora, application-specific attack synthesis, customer correction traces, and low-latency enforcement. The biggest validation gap is third-party measurement: the "2x" and "$28M" claims need methodology, baselines, and incident severity definitions.

**Founders.** Aum Upadhyay says he built an AWS security and privacy framework that prevented more than $1.8 billion in damages. Eduardo Velasco says he chained prompt injection into root access inside ChatGPT and previously led low-latency ML systems at Amazon. This is unusually relevant security and systems experience, though enterprise buyer references remain undisclosed.

**Verdict — 8.1/10, Medium confidence.** Silmaril targets a real blocker for agent adoption and frames the problem correctly around harmful execution. Confidence depends on audited benchmarks, customer evidence, latency under load, and proof that self-improvement does not overfit toy exploits.

**Sources:** [YC profile and launch, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/silmaril) · [Company site, accessed June 23, 2026](https://silmaril.dev)

## 3. PerfectBit

**Money.** PerfectBit is a YC Spring 2026 company with no disclosed additional financing, customer, revenue, dataset volume, or licensing model. Its public evidence is thesis-heavy: it is building correct-by-construction AI training data rather than merely scraping more code or human labels. [YC profile, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/perfectbit-inc)

**Technology — disclosed.** PerfectBit describes itself as producing "correct by construction AI training data." Public YC text is sparse, so the technical assessment must be inferential and conservative. The likely target is model training data whose correctness can be verified mechanically through compilers, tests, solvers, proofs, or constrained generation rather than subjective labeling.

**Technology — assessment.** The core workflow likely starts with a formal or executable specification, generates candidate tasks and solutions, and accepts only examples that pass a verifier. In coding, that might mean type checking, unit tests, property tests, fuzzing, symbolic execution, or proof obligations. In math or reasoning, it might mean SMT solvers, theorem provers, or generated counterexamples. The key is not that data is synthetic; it is that labels have a machine-checkable correctness boundary.

The hardest engineering is preventing verifier overfitting. Models trained on generated tasks can learn shortcuts, exploit weak tests, or become excellent at artificial distributions that do not transfer to real work. A serious pipeline needs diverse task generators, adversarial test construction, hidden validators, difficulty calibration, contamination controls, and metadata that describes what skill each example exercises. If the company serves frontier labs, data provenance, rights, and uniqueness matter as much as correctness. Commodity pieces include code runners, theorem provers, and LLM-based generation. A moat could come from generator quality, verification depth, curriculum metadata, and empirical evidence that training on PerfectBit data improves held-out real benchmarks. The biggest gap is absence of public benchmarks, domains, dataset sizes, or customer proof.

**Founders.** YC does not expose enough founder detail in the public profile to evaluate a strong founder-market fit. That is a material gap for a company whose value depends on deep verification, ML training, and data distribution into sophisticated AI labs.

**Verdict — 7.9/10, Medium confidence.** The thesis is technically important: verified data is more valuable than larger untrusted corpora for coding and reasoning models. The score is capped until PerfectBit publishes domains, verifiers, benchmark lifts, and customer or lab adoption.

**Sources:** [YC profile, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/perfectbit-inc)

## 4. Prototyping.io

[YC profile, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/prototypingio)

**Technology — assessment.** A credible autonomous manufacturing stack needs to ingest CAD files, drawings, tolerances, material requirements, finishes, inspection requirements, and delivery constraints. It then has to classify manufacturability across CNC machining, sheet metal, additive, casting, or hybrid processes; identify risky features; estimate setup and cycle time; price material and labor; route to internal or partner capacity; generate manufacturing instructions; and produce inspection plans.

If the system uses partner shops, it also needs supplier capability models, capacity freshness, quality history, and feedback from defects. Defensibility could come from a geometry-to-process model, quote-to-actual feedback, machine/supplier graph, and accumulated manufacturability corrections. **Founders.** YC's public profile does not provide enough founder background to assess manufacturing depth. For this category, missing founder and operations evidence matters because execution depends on both software and physical quality systems.

**Verdict — 7.8/10, Medium confidence.** The workflow is technically rich and commercially clear, but public evidence is sparse. The rating depends on demonstrated quote accuracy, gross margin, inspection outcomes, supplier reliability, and repeat usage by hardware teams.

**Verdict — 7.7/10, Medium confidence.** The category is high-upside and technically deep, but the evidence is mostly a directional thesis. ## 6. StableBrowse

**Money.** StableBrowse has YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, customer count, revenue, request volume, retention, or benchmarked reliability. It has a clear developer-infrastructure wedge because brittle browser automation is a common failure mode for agents. [YC profile, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/stablebrowse)

**Technology — disclosed.** StableBrowse is a "browser layer for AI agents." Public detail is limited, but the name and category imply infrastructure that lets agents use web applications more reliably than raw browser-control scripts.

**Technology — assessment.** A robust agent browser layer needs stateful sessions, DOM and visual observations, action abstraction, credential isolation, replay logs, network controls, rate limits, and deterministic recovery after page changes. A higher-level layer can expose semantic actions such as "download invoice," "fill claim form," or "find pricing table" rather than raw click coordinates. To be useful, it must combine browser execution with task assertions, page-state normalization, anti-flakiness logic, and safe handling of CAPTCHAs, auth, file downloads, and private data.

The hard problem is adversarial and changing UIs. Web apps shift DOM structure, lazy-load content, hide actions behind modals, and sometimes block automation. LLM agents also suffer from prompt injection in page text. StableBrowse should therefore isolate browser state per task, sanitize observations, constrain outbound actions, and record every click, input, response, and downloaded artifact. Commodity components include Playwright, Browserbase-style hosted browsers, screenshot models, and RPA frameworks. Defensibility would come from a task-level action model, reliability telemetry across many sites, replay/eval infrastructure, and security controls that enterprises can trust. Missing evidence includes uptime, task success rates, latency, cost per browser-hour, and named agent-platform users.

**Founders.** YC's public profile does not give enough founder background to verify deep browser-infrastructure or security experience. That is a meaningful gap because this category demands operational reliability more than a demo.

**Verdict — 7.6/10, Medium confidence.** StableBrowse is pointed at a real layer below agents. The rating depends on measured task-completion uplift, session isolation, anti-injection safeguards, cost, and integration into production agent stacks.

## 7. Tenet Industries

**Money.** Tenet has YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, government contract, paid pilot, production order, export approval, or field deployment. Its public evidence is prototype progress: in four months it says it built custom flight-computer hardware, custom flight software, an encrypted ultra-broadband frequency-hopping system, and a kamikaze quadcopter that assembles in under two minutes with half the parts of comparable systems. [YC profile and launch, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/tenet-industries)

**Technology — disclosed.** Tenet designs low-cost mass-producible defense systems, starting with attack drones. Its approach reverses the typical defense process: start with manufacturing constraints and target unit economics, then design hardware and software backward from those constraints. The first product, Midsummer, is a low-cost strike quadcopter.

**Technology — assessment.** Tenet's technical bet is that software can compensate for cheaper hardware. A manufacturing-first drone stack requires simplified mechanical design, low-part-count electronics, rapid assembly fixtures, firmware that tolerates component variance, communications resilient to interference, and flight control that performs acceptably despite lower-cost sensors and materials. The stated one-PCB architecture can reduce assembly time and failure points, but it concentrates thermal, EMI, repair, and supply-chain risk.

The hard engineering is not a flying prototype; it is repeatable production under jamming, weather, payload variation, and operator stress. Defense buyers also require safety, cybersecurity, supply-chain traceability, export-control compliance, and logistics. Commodity pieces include open-source flight controllers, FPV frames, commodity radios, and computer-vision/autonomy modules. A moat could emerge from manufacturing process IP, costed designs, flight data, supplier relationships, and procurement credibility. Validation gaps are severe: no unit cost, endurance, payload, range, jam resistance, failure rate, production capacity, or customer evidence is public.

**Founders.** Public founder descriptions are thin but mission-specific: Emil Falk focuses on low-cost hyperscaling of strike systems, Fabian Andersson is CTO, and Hugo Frisk focuses on mass production. The apparent bias toward manufacturing is aligned with the thesis, but there is no public evidence yet of defense procurement, certification, or combat-field deployment experience.

**Verdict — 7.5/10, Medium confidence.** Tenet attacks a real cost-asymmetry problem with the right manufacturing-first framing. The score is capped by procurement risk, safety, export controls, field reliability, and absent contract evidence.

## 8. Atrisa

**Money.** Atrisa has YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, customer, revenue, paid pilot, chip tape-out, PDK partnership, or design-win evidence. It is technically ambitious but commercially unvalidated in public. [YC profile and launch, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/atrisa)

**Technology — disclosed.** Atrisa builds AI agents for analog circuit design. It claims the system reasons hierarchically about circuits, finds topologies matching specifications, performs debugging and experimentation, accounts for reuse, interference, parasitics, and physical-layout context, and can work with existing toolkits, PDKs, and design documents.

**Technology — assessment.** Analog design is a strong target because expert reasoning is scarce, specs are multi-objective, and simulation loops are expensive. A credible system needs to translate requirements into topology choices, size devices, set bias points, run SPICE simulations, inspect waveforms, optimize for gain/noise/power/area/stability/yield, and account for process corners, parasitics, and layout constraints. It should interact with EDA tools and preserve every design decision, simulation condition, and failed attempt.

The hard problem is physical validity. An LLM can describe a plausible op-amp; getting a robust circuit across PVT corners and layout parasitics is different. Search needs circuit priors, constraint solvers, simulation orchestration, and active learning over expensive runs. Debugging requires causal diagnosis: instability, mismatch, headroom, layout coupling, device stress, or bad testbench setup. Commodity components include EDA scripting, Bayesian optimization, SPICE, and language interfaces. Defensibility could come from proprietary design traces, PDK-specific evaluations, expert correction loops, and integration into existing analog workflows. The validation gap is whether Atrisa can produce manufacturable designs, not only assist conversation.

**Founders.** Sayan Mitra previously worked as an AI research engineer at KLA Semiconductors and studied electrical engineering at IIT Madras. Atman Kar was a Texas Instruments engineer. Rithik Jain focuses on GTM and customer experience. This is a credible semiconductor-software founding mix.

**Verdict — 7.4/10, Medium confidence.** Atrisa has a deep technical wedge and relevant founder experience. The required proof is benchmarked circuit synthesis, simulation pass rates, human time saved, tape-out evidence, and PDK/security handling.

## 10. Totalis

**Money.** Totalis has YC seed financing and no disclosed outside round, trading volume, active user count, fee revenue, market-maker relationship, regulatory approval, or custody structure. It is an infrastructure bet rather than a traction story. [YC profile, accessed June 23, 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/totalis)

**Technology — disclosed.** Totalis describes itself as the derivatives layer for prediction markets. Public detail is minimal, so the analysis must infer cautiously from the category: derivative instruments built on event-market outcomes, likely enabling leverage, hedging, structured exposure, or cross-market strategies.

**Technology — assessment.** A derivatives layer has to model the underlying prediction contract precisely. That means contract wording, resolution source, deadline, void rules, jurisdiction, settlement currency, fees, liquidity, and correlation with adjacent contracts. Derivative pricing must account for binary-event probability, time to resolution, liquidity, oracle/resolution risk, venue outages, margin, and hedging cost. Execution infrastructure needs order management, risk limits, mark-to-market, liquidation logic, and audit trails.

The hardest problem is basis and resolution risk. Two markets can look economically similar but resolve differently, and a derivative written on top of an event contract magnifies wording ambiguity. Liquidity can disappear around news, and manipulation risk is non-trivial in thin markets. Regulatory structure also matters: event contracts, swaps, options, and leveraged products trigger different regimes. Commodity pieces include exchange adapters, portfolio margin systems, and pricing models. A moat could emerge from contract-normalization data, risk models, execution relationships, and market-maker liquidity. Public evidence is thin: without volume, legal structure, or product definitions, the company remains high-potential but speculative.

**Founders.** YC's public page does not expose enough founder history for a firm assessment. For market infrastructure, missing evidence on trading systems, compliance, and risk management is important.

**Verdict — 7.1/10, Medium-low confidence.** Totalis attacks a plausible next layer in event markets, but the risk surface is large. Confidence requires legal clarity, product specs, live liquidity, margin performance, resolution-risk controls, and audited trading volume.
