## 1. Origami Robotics

**Money.** Origami Robotics is a YC Winter 2026 company, so the verified baseline financing is YC's standard $500,000 deal. The strongest customer signal is company-reported: its YC launch post says it had already sold robotic hands to physical-AI labs including Amazon. It does not disclose revenue, unit price, margins, backlog, manufacturing capacity, or service contracts. The company is still early, but hardware sales to research labs are a better signal than a pure demo because manipulation hardware has to survive repeated use.

**Technology.** Origami builds a robotic manipulation stack around a high-degree-of-freedom robotic hand and a matching data-collection glove. A robotics lab, warehouse operator, or manufacturer would use the glove/device to collect human-like manipulation demonstrations. The system records finger motion, contacts, forces, timing, and task context; maps that data to the robotic hand because the hand and glove are designed to match; trains manipulation policies; and deploys them on the robot hand. Humans still define tasks, reset failed trials, inspect data quality, approve deployment settings, and handle unsafe or uncertain actions.

The disclosed technical argument is unusually concrete: Origami says existing robot hands suffer from embodiment gap and poor dynamics because human-hand data does not transfer cleanly to robot hardware. Its blog argues that high-ratio gearboxes hurt sim-to-real transfer, force transparency, backdrivability, durability, and sensing. The company claims to move from high-ratio transmission toward lower-ratio, direct-drive style hand actuation with in-joint motors, then pairs that with a data glove that has matching kinematics. Analyst inference: the moat would be the combined hardware/data loop, not a single model. If Origami can deploy identical or near-identical hands across factories and labs, every customer interaction can become training data for grasping, folding, tool use, cable handling, and assembly.

The hard engineering is brutal: miniature motors, thermal limits, force sensing, tactile feedback, calibration drift, safety, data labeling, policy evaluation, and manufacturability. Commodity robotics policies will not solve the contact physics alone. Origami has a credible wedge because it attacks the data collection bottleneck and the hardware embodiment bottleneck together. The gap is validation outside lab tasks: public material does not yet show cycle-time improvement, failure rate, lifetime, or cost per deployed hand.

**Founders.** Quanting "Daniel" Xie is a roboticist from CMU Robotics Institute. Ryan Xie is described as a serial hardware founder. That is strong founder-market fit for a hardware-first physical-AI company.

**Verdict.** Deep technical upside with a real hardware/data thesis; manufacturing scale and field reliability are the main unknowns. Rating: 8.3/10. Confidence: medium-high.

## 2. Coasty

**Money.** Coasty is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing as the only verified outside capital. It discloses public pricing rather than revenue: a Starter plan at $19/month and an Unlimited plan at $99/month appear on its website. No paid customer count, retention, enterprise contract, or gross margin data is public. The strongest traction signal is technical: the company claims an 82% OSWorld completion rate across 369 real-world tasks.

**Technology.** Coasty is a computer-use agent platform. A user gives the system a task such as updating a spreadsheet, filling a web form, operating an old desktop application, or running a browser workflow. The product sees the screen, plans the next action, clicks, types, scrolls, drags, checks the result, and repeats until the task is done or needs help. The API docs describe a loop where the customer sends a screenshot and natural-language instruction, receives pixel-level actions, executes them, captures another screenshot, and continues. Coasty also exposes sessions, managed machines, task runs, streaming events, webhooks, workflows, and human takeover.

The hard part is not just screen parsing. Real business workflows include slow pages, surprise pop-ups, permission dialogs, changed menus, broken focus, CAPTCHAs, and legacy UI state that is not represented in HTML. Coasty's pitch is that it reads the screen rather than relying on brittle selectors, self-checks its own output, leaves replayable timestamps, and flags what it cannot fix. Analyst inference: the architecture likely combines vision-language models, grounding, stateful VM sessions, action-verification loops, retry policies, workflow DSL constraints, and human-intervention hooks. The moat would come from reliability data across messy workflows and from operating infrastructure that keeps long-running sessions stable.

The risk is benchmark transfer. OSWorld is useful, but enterprise RPA replacement requires extremely low silent-error rates, audit logs, credential controls, and predictable costs. A $99 unlimited tier is attractive but could become expensive to support if users run high-latency desktop tasks constantly.

**Founders.** Prateek Jannu and Nitish Kovuru are listed as founders. Kovuru cites Columbia CS, Purdue computer engineering, graduate AI TA work, and prior enterprise sales automation agent work. That maps well to computer-use reliability.

**Verdict.** Strong agent-infrastructure wedge if the 82% reliability claim holds in customer workflows and human takeover is clean. Rating: 8.2/10. Confidence: medium-high.

## 3. Aurorin CAD

**Money.** Aurorin CAD is a YC Winter 2026 company with YC seed financing. It has a public product surface and launch post, but no disclosed ARR, paid-seat count, enterprise pilots, pricing, or retention. The strongest evidence is product depth: it is building a new CAD system rather than a plugin, and it offers Mac/Windows downloads.

**Technology.** Aurorin is mechanical CAD software for engineers designing parts and assemblies. A mechanical engineer opens or creates a model, describes geometry changes in natural language or uses conventional CAD interactions, and the system updates features, constraints, surfaces, dimensions, and assembly state. The output is a parametric model that can be inspected, edited, exported, and used in downstream engineering. A human engineer remains responsible for tolerances, manufacturability, safety, simulation assumptions, and final release.

The technical bet is to rebuild the CAD kernel around AI and modern hardware. The YC profile and launch material say most mainstream CAD products depend on older kernels, that complex assemblies can take hours to open, and that Aurorin uses a custom parametric and B-Rep-driven kernel designed for modern CPUs/GPUs. That matters because AI cannot reliably modify geometry if the underlying kernel is slow, opaque, or brittle. Analyst inference: Aurorin likely needs geometric constraint solving, boundary-representation operations, feature history, robust topology naming, file import/export, GPU-accelerated visualization, and a model-editing agent that can call deterministic CAD operations rather than "draw" geometry probabilistically.

The hard engineering is kernel correctness. CAD users tolerate annoying UI, but they cannot tolerate invalid surfaces, broken references, invisible tolerancing errors, or exported geometry that fails manufacturing. The moat would be a kernel-plus-agent stack where every generated feature remains editable and auditable. The competition is severe: SolidWorks, Onshape, Fusion, NX, Creo, and startups adding AI on top of existing kernels.

**Founders.** Michael Baron is the solo founder. YC lists three SpaceX internships across Raptor combustion simulation, Dragon guidance/navigation/control, and Starshield flight software, plus Apple GPU driver performance. That is strong founder-technology fit for CAD performance and engineering tools.

**Verdict.** Ambitious and technically serious; kernel quality and compatibility will decide whether it becomes a tool or only an impressive demo. Rating: 8.1/10. Confidence: medium.

**Technology.** Isengard builds mass-producible AI-powered precision munitions and counter-UAS systems for allied nations. The customer is a defense buyer or allied production partner. The input is an operational need: range, payload, target type, navigation constraints, local manufacturing capacity, and cost per effect. Isengard's system portfolio maps those needs to airframes, propulsion, encrypted datalink, visual navigation, targeting, production process, and support. Humans remain central: military users set rules of engagement, authorize use, inspect hardware, control procurement, and validate compliance.

Public material is intentionally high level but specific enough to analyze the system shape. Analyst inference: the hard stack includes airframe design, guidance/navigation/control, onboard perception, secure communications, target recognition, propulsion integration, production quality control, and field maintenance. The differentiation is not just "AI drones"; it is designing for local high-volume production and lower cost per effect.

Risks are unusually high: export controls, safety, procurement cycles, combat validation, liability, supply chain constraints, and public scrutiny. **Verdict.** High-conviction defense manufacturing wedge with real founder-market fit; current company proof remains early. Rating: 8.0/10. Confidence: medium.

## 5. Condor Energy

**Money.** Condor Energy is a YC Winter 2026 company with YC seed financing. Its YC launch material claims it is live with a leading data-center operator, large retail chains, and European industrial companies, and that customers can cut electricity bills by up to 20%. It does not disclose ARR, contract values, gross margin, verified customer names, or savings audits.

**Technology.** Condor serves large electricity consumers such as industrial sites, retailers, and data centers. The customer connects meter data, site loads, utility tariffs, supply contracts, PPAs, solar PV, batteries, market prices, regulation, hedges, and operational constraints. Condor builds a live view of consumption, unit cost, hedging coverage, and exposure; forecasts risk; recommends procurement and hedging actions; and helps energy teams decide when to buy, hedge, shift load, or invest in flexibility. Humans approve actual trades, contracts, load changes, and capital decisions.

The product is valuable because enterprise power cost is no longer a simple price-times-volume problem. Data centers and industrial users face location, time-of-day, peak, imbalance, grid-fee, tax, and regulatory effects. Analyst inference: Condor likely needs time-series ingestion, tariff modeling, scenario simulation, market-data feeds, contract parsing, optimization over hedge products, and alerting when operational behavior creates cost risk. The hard part is trust: a bad hedge or load-shifting recommendation can cost millions. The system must explain its assumptions, show sensitivity to price/weather/load changes, and preserve audit trails.

Commodity dashboards are easy. The moat would be energy-market expertise embedded in software, customer-specific load models, and closed-loop proof of savings. The main gap is independent validation of the claimed 20% savings and whether Condor can operate across countries with different market structures.

**Founders.** The founders describe backgrounds in electricity trading, hydropower, energy physics, and quantitative finance. Florian Perocheau is listed as a former equity quant and power trader. This is directly relevant founder-market fit.

**Verdict.** Strong vertical software for a growing data-center power problem; proof depends on audited savings and market coverage. Rating: 7.9/10. Confidence: medium.

## 6. LATO

**Money.** LATO is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing. It does not disclose ARR or contract values. The website shows investor testimonials and positions the product against expert calls, surveys, and commercial diligence studies that can cost hundreds of thousands of dollars. That is buyer-specific evidence, but not revenue proof.

**Technology.** LATO is a market-research and simulation platform for investors. A private-equity, venture, or growth investor enters an investment question: market size, customer pain, pricing power, competitor behavior, churn risk, regional adoption, or diligence on a target. LATO combines public sources, the fund's own data, and first-hand interviews with customers, experts, and operators. It then builds a research report and a simulation of market participants. The output is a cited diligence artifact with findings, interview evidence, and simulated behavior. Human investors still scope the study, approve interview targets, interpret conflicting evidence, and make investment decisions.

The workflow has three technical layers. First is sourcing: finding relevant customers, experts, operators, and documents. LATO says it can use 4 million verified people and run studies with voice interviews. Second is synthesis: converting interviews and documents into themes, metrics, and cited claims. Third is simulation: building "digital twins" or market participants to test scenarios. Analyst inference: the stack likely includes entity/person search, interview orchestration, transcript analysis, retrieval, source citation, segment clustering, and agent-based simulation. The risky part is calibration. Simulated market participants can produce confident nonsense if interviews are biased or too sparse.

LATO's moat would come from a proprietary panel, study templates, and accumulated diligence benchmarks by sector. The largest validation gap is whether simulated outcomes predict real market behavior better than a good consultant's judgment.

**Founders.** Tien Chu has ML/SWE experience across enterprise R&D, research labs, and startups. Tymek Staniszewski spent six years in growth and private equity at Verdane, with $11 billion AUM cited in his profile. That gives LATO direct buyer understanding.

**Verdict.** Useful if it compresses diligence from weeks to hours without losing source discipline. Rating: 7.8/10. Confidence: medium.

## 7. Insurf

**Money.** Insurf is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing. It does not disclose revenue, practice count, broker partners, or insurer relationships. The website advertises a 90-day Access Recovery pilot for specialty practices, which is the current commercial signal.

**Technology.** Insurf builds a data and decision layer for health insurance. The first product, Inveto, starts when a healthcare claim or prior authorization is denied. A practice supplies the denial, clinical chart, payer policy, and medical context. Inveto turns that into a source-cited, physician-attested appeal packet, tracks the outcome, and records the payer behavior. The second product, Surely, uses those coverage outcomes and plan details to estimate the true 12-month cost of a health plan for an individual, employer, or broker, including premiums, deductibles, copays, drug tiers, networks, and out-of-pocket limits. Humans review clinical facts, physician attestation, patient communication, and final appeals.

The technical core is a coverage-decision graph. Analyst inference: Insurf needs medical-record extraction, denial classification, payer-policy retrieval, citation generation, clinical-rule checking, outcome tracking, plan-cost simulation, and strict PHI controls. The appeal generator must be evidence-grounded because unsupported medical claims can harm patients and practices. The plan-cost engine must model real doctors, drugs, and network constraints, not generic actuarial averages.

The moat could compound if every appeal outcome becomes a rights-cleared row in a payer behavior dataset. The main gaps are privacy/security proof, payer coverage, appeal win rates, physician time saved, and whether payer behavior changes faster than the graph can learn.

**Founders.** Bryan Chung cites international physics ranking, USACO Platinum, and IEEE publication. Chang Lu is listed as ex-Brown BS/MD with neuroscience/economics background and zero-shot modeling research at Emory Winship Cancer Institute. The team has relevant technical and healthcare context, though insurance operations depth remains to be proven.

**Verdict.** Strong workflow and data-compounding thesis in a painful healthcare area; needs outcome evidence. Rating: 7.8/10. Confidence: medium.

## 8. Blueprints

**Money.** Blueprints is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing. It discloses no revenue, user count, assets traded, take rate, or broker/dealer structure. The public product is a strategy builder for Polymarket workflows, so regulatory, market-venue, and custody details are important open questions.

**Technology.** Blueprints lets a user build automated prediction-market strategies. A trader starts with a thesis in plain English. The "Prince" AI researches the market and live data, converts the thesis into a visual no-code flow, backtests the logic against Polymarket markets since 2022, paper-trades with simulated funds, and then deploys live to co-located infrastructure with risk controls gating orders. Humans review the strategy, limits, market assumptions, execution rules, and final deployment.

The product is not just a chatbot over prediction markets. It combines research, strategy graph construction, historical replay, paper trading, alerts, order execution, and infrastructure. Analyst inference: the architecture likely includes market-data ingestion, historical order book or event replay, strategy DAGs, simulation, slippage/fee modeling, wallet/exchange integration, risk limits, and audit logs. The hard part is avoiding false backtests. Prediction markets are thin, event definitions change, liquidity disappears, and fees/slippage can erase edge.

The moat could be workflow speed for traders who understand a market but cannot code execution systems. Competition includes quant platforms, Polymarket-native tools, and general agent frameworks. Blueprints needs proof that generated strategies survive live trading, not just backtests.

**Founders.** Ryan Morrissey is listed as CEO with prior Stripe and Induct experience. Bence Redmond is CTO with prior AWS experience. That is relevant for payments/infrastructure but public market-trading depth is not yet clear.

**Verdict.** Clear product for an emerging market, with real infra requirements; live performance and compliance are the key risks. Rating: 7.6/10. Confidence: medium.

## 9. Tsenta

**Money.** Tsenta is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing. It does not disclose revenue, paid users, application volume, or placement rate. It has a public product site, pricing surface, and YC job post. The founders say they built it after manually applying to more than 3,000 jobs.

**Technology.** Tsenta is a job-search automation agent. A job seeker gives it a profile, resume, preferences, target roles, accounts, and approval settings. Tsenta monitors more than 50,000 career pages across Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, Ashby, and other ATSs; matches roles to the user; tailors an ATS-safe resume and cover material; shows changes; fills application forms through a web app, iMessage bot, MCP, Chrome extension, or other interface; and tracks status. Humans review materials, approve sensitive submissions, handle logins or anti-bot failures, and correct profile facts.

The workflow is simple but brittle. Applicant portals differ, change fields, add knockout questions, require account creation, and sometimes penalize bad automation. Analyst inference: Tsenta needs career-page crawling, deduplication, job matching, resume tailoring, browser automation, field mapping, login/session handling, anti-duplication controls, and a tracker. The best product constraint is user control: fully black-box auto-apply can hurt applicants if it submits weak or inaccurate forms. Tsenta claims transparency and on-device automation; that matters because credentials and resumes are sensitive.

The moat is not the base model. It is ATS coverage, role-matching quality, form automation reliability, and trust with job seekers. The risk is platform hostility and low-quality mass application behavior that recruiters may filter.

**Founders.** Agnay Srivastava and Pulkit Gupta are Rose-Hulman CS students with internships and applied software backgrounds. Their founder-market fit comes from firsthand international-student job-search pain rather than years in recruiting.

**Verdict.** Useful painkiller if it stays controlled and accurate; risks becoming spam automation if incentives drift. Rating: 7.5/10. Confidence: medium.

## 10. Parasma

**Money.** Parasma is a YC Summer 2026 company with YC seed financing. It does not disclose revenue, lab customers, grant funding, patents, hardware partners, or compute benchmarks. Public evidence is early and research-oriented: the website says the company writes algorithms to turn brain cells into compute, and its June 18 note says YC is backing it.

**Technology.** Parasma is attempting biological compute with living neural systems. A researcher would culture or access neural tissue, connect it to a sensing/stimulation interface, run training algorithms that map signals and feedback to task performance, and measure whether the biological system performs useful computation with lower energy than silicon. The YC profile says founder Sean Cole made human brain cells play Doom. Humans supervise biological protocols, ethical safeguards, stimulation limits, task design, and interpretation of outputs.

Public implementation details are extremely sparse, so the following is analyst inference. A plausible stack includes multi-electrode arrays or similar neural interfaces, real-time signal decoding, stimulation policies, reward feedback, closed-loop training, viability monitoring, and software that maps task states to stimulation and neural activity back to actions. The hard science is not "making cells play a game" once; it is getting repeatable, scalable, controllable, and ethically acceptable computation from living tissue. Parasma's research page explicitly flags consciousness and suffering safeguards, which is important because this area carries moral risk in addition to technical uncertainty.

The moat could be algorithms and protocols for training living neural systems efficiently. The validation gaps are enormous: energy-per-useful-computation, reproducibility, lifespan, input/output bandwidth, manufacturing, contamination risk, ethics review, and whether biological systems can compete with specialized silicon.

**Founders.** Sean Cole is a solo founder with an MS in Artificial Intelligence from the University of Sussex. The YC profile and company note credit prior influence from Cortical Labs and Sussex collaborators. The founder has unusual relevance, but solo-founder bio-compute is a high-risk path.

**Verdict.** Very high scientific upside, but the public evidence is still early and hard to evaluate. Rating: 7.4/10. Confidence: low-medium.
